Overview

Competition Format & Structure

F Format
  • Team competition: Each country sends a team of up to 5 members
  • Day 1 — Preparation: Teams receive the case, analyze, and build slides
  • Day 2 — Presentation: Present solution to a panel of judges + Q&A
  • Language: All presentations must be in English
  • Slides submitted by deadline — no changes after submission
S Scoring (50 pts max final)
  • Raw scores per criterion → median of judges' scores
  • Normalized via Z-score formula: Zi = (Gi − A) / σ
  • Final score: Fi = 25 × (1 + Zi/2)
  • Group winners advance to public Business Case Finals
  • Final result = max(group stage, finals)

Core Skills

The 4 Competencies Judges Evaluate

1 Analytical Thinking

Structure a complex business problem into clear, logical streams.

  • Break the case into large blocks → sub-blocks → specific problems
  • Apply the MECE principle: Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive
  • No overlap between streams, but all possibilities are covered
  • Use issue trees, decision trees, or logic frameworks
2 Conceptual Thinking

Build correct hypotheses and validate them logically.

  • Generate hypotheses based on your analytical structure
  • Check: Does the idea address the actual problem?
  • Verify feasibility — is the solution implementable?
  • Apply business sense — does it make commercial/strategic sense?
3 Quantitative Thinking

Back up your recommendations with numbers.

  • Simple but fast calculations (market sizing, revenue projections)
  • Build models to illustrate analysis (cost-benefit, break-even)
  • Use data from the case — don't ignore provided numbers
  • Sanity check: Do your numbers make sense in context?
4 Communication Skills

Present your solution clearly and persuasively.

  • Build a logical presentation flow (situation → analysis → recommendation)
  • Answer judges' questions directly and concisely
  • All team members should speak and contribute
  • Visual aids: clear charts, minimal text on slides

Judges' perspective: They are looking for structured, feasible, quantified solutions — not just creative ideas. A mediocre idea with excellent structure scores higher than a brilliant idea with poor logic.

Framework 1

MECE Principle & Problem Structuring

M MECE Explained

Mutually Exclusive: No overlap between categories

Collectively Exhaustive: All possibilities covered

Example: "How to increase profit?"

MECE split: Increase Revenue OR Decrease Costs

Revenue = Price × Quantity (no overlap, covers all)

Costs = Fixed + Variable (no overlap, covers all)

I Issue Tree

Break the central question into sub-questions hierarchically:

  • Level 1: Main question → 2–4 major branches
  • Level 2: Each branch → 2–3 sub-branches
  • Level 3: Specific, actionable questions
  • Each level should be MECE
P The Pyramid Principle (Barbara Minto)
  • Start with the answer — lead with your recommendation
  • Group supporting arguments below the answer
  • Each argument is supported by evidence/data
  • Top-down structure: conclusion → reasons → evidence

SCR Framework: Situation (context) → Complication (problem) → Resolution (recommendation). Use this to open your presentation.

A Anti-Patterns to Avoid
  • ❌ Listing ideas without structure ("brainstorm dump")
  • ❌ Overlapping categories (violates ME)
  • ❌ Missing obvious options (violates CE)
  • ❌ Going too deep on one branch, ignoring others
  • ❌ Burying the recommendation at the end
Framework 2

Essential Business Frameworks

P Porter's Five Forces

Industry attractiveness analysis:

  • Threat of new entrants: Barriers to entry (capital, regulation, brand)
  • Bargaining power of suppliers: How many? Switching costs?
  • Bargaining power of buyers: Price sensitivity, alternatives
  • Threat of substitutes: Alternative products/services
  • Competitive rivalry: Number and strength of competitors
S SWOT Analysis
  • Strengths: Internal advantages (brand, tech, team)
  • Weaknesses: Internal disadvantages (costs, gaps)
  • Opportunities: External favorable conditions (market growth, trends)
  • Threats: External risks (regulation, competition, macro)

Use as a starting point, not the entire analysis.

4 4Ps / Marketing Mix
  • Product: What are we selling? Features, quality, differentiation
  • Price: Pricing strategy (penetration, skimming, cost-plus, value-based)
  • Place: Distribution channels (direct, retail, online, partnerships)
  • Promotion: How to reach customers (digital, influencers, PR, events)
M More Useful Frameworks
  • PESTEL: Political, Economic, Social, Tech, Environmental, Legal — macro environment scan
  • Value Chain: Primary vs. support activities — find cost/value advantages
  • BCG Matrix: Stars, Cash Cows, Question Marks, Dogs — portfolio analysis
  • Break-even: FC / (Price − VC per unit) — when does the business become profitable?

IEO Tip: Don't just name-drop frameworks. Apply them to the specific case with data. Judges reward relevant application, not textbook recitation.

Framework 3

Quantitative Analysis & Market Sizing

M Market Sizing (Fermi Estimation)

Estimate market size when data is limited:

  • Top-down: Total market → segment share → target
  • Bottom-up: # customers × spend per customer × frequency
  • State your assumptions explicitly
  • Use round numbers — precision isn't the point, logic is

Example: Coffee shop revenue in Hanoi

Population 8M × 40% drink coffee × 5 cups/week × 30k VND × 52 weeks ÷ 10,000 shops = ~500M VND/shop/year

B Break-Even Analysis

BEP (units) = Fixed Costs / (Price − Variable Cost per unit)

BEP (revenue) = Fixed Costs / Contribution Margin Ratio

Always calculate: How long until the proposed investment pays off?

P Profitability Analysis
  • Revenue: Price × Volume. Can we increase either?
  • Gross margin: (Revenue − COGS) / Revenue
  • Operating margin: (Revenue − COGS − OpEx) / Revenue
  • Unit economics: Revenue per unit − Cost per unit
  • CAC vs LTV: Customer Acquisition Cost vs. Lifetime Value (LTV > 3× CAC is healthy)
I Impact Estimation
  • Quantify the impact of each recommendation
  • Use scenarios: Conservative / Base / Optimistic
  • Show sensitivity analysis — what if assumptions change?
  • Timeline: Short-term wins vs. long-term investments

Judge's perspective: "Show me the numbers." Even rough estimates show analytical rigor. A recommendation without quantification is just an opinion.

Execution

Slide Design & Presentation Delivery

S Slide Design Principles
  • One message per slide — use the slide title as the takeaway
  • Action titles: "Revenue will grow 25% by 2026" not "Revenue Analysis"
  • Minimal text: Bullets, not paragraphs. Speak the details.
  • Visual hierarchy: Most important info is largest/most prominent
  • Charts > Tables > Text for data presentation

D Delivery Tips
  • All members speak — assign sections based on strength
  • Rehearse transitions between speakers (smooth handoffs)
  • Time management: Practice to fit the allotted time
  • Eye contact with judges, not the screen
  • Speak slowly and clearly — English is a second language for most
Q Handling Q&A
  • Listen fully before answering — don't interrupt the judge
  • Restate the question briefly to confirm understanding
  • Answer directly first, then elaborate if needed
  • It's OK to say: "That's a great point we didn't fully explore, but our initial thinking is..."
  • Designate a Q&A lead who can redirect to the right team member

Pro tip: Judges often test your assumptions during Q&A. Prepare a list of your key assumptions and be ready to defend or adjust them.

Case Types

Common Business Case Scenarios

1 Market Entry

"Should company X enter market Y?"

  • Market size & growth analysis
  • Competitive landscape (Porter's 5 Forces)
  • Entry mode: organic, acquisition, partnership?
  • Financial projection & break-even timeline
2 Growth Strategy

"How should company X grow revenue by Z%?"

  • Ansoff Matrix: market penetration, development, product development, diversification
  • Organic vs. inorganic growth (M&A)
  • Customer segment analysis
  • Quantify revenue impact of each option
3 Turnaround / Cost Reduction

"Company X is losing money. What should they do?"

  • Diagnose: Revenue problem or cost problem?
  • Cost structure analysis (fixed vs variable)
  • Revenue optimization (pricing, mix, churn)
  • Prioritize by impact and feasibility
4 Social Impact / Policy

"Design a solution for economic/social challenge Z."

  • Stakeholder analysis (who benefits, who pays)
  • Market failure identification (externalities, public goods)
  • Policy tools: taxes, subsidies, regulation, education
  • Implementation plan with measurable KPIs

Past IEO cases: Have included sustainability/ESG themes, fintech disruption, post-COVID recovery, and emerging market challenges. Study past cases on the IEO website.

Team Work

Team Strategy & Preparation Day

R Role Assignment
  • Team Lead: Facilitates discussion, manages time, makes final calls
  • Analyst: Deep-dives into data, builds models, runs numbers
  • Strategist: Develops the recommendation, evaluates options
  • Slide Designer: Builds the presentation, ensures visual quality
  • Q&A Lead: Prepares for judge questions, coordinates responses

Roles can overlap — in a team of 5, everyone does multiple things.

T Time Allocation (Prep Day)
  • First 20%: Read case thoroughly (everyone). Discuss, ask questions.
  • Next 10%: Structure the problem. Agree on MECE framework.
  • Next 30%: Deep analysis in parallel (split work by branch).
  • Next 20%: Synthesize findings. Develop recommendation.
  • Last 20%: Build slides. Rehearse. Polish.
P Pre-Competition Preparation
  • Practice with past IEO business cases (2018–2024 on website)
  • Do timed mock presentations — simulate the real constraint
  • Build a slide template in advance (colors, fonts, chart styles)
  • Prepare a framework cheat sheet (Porter's, SWOT, 4Ps, break-even formulas)
  • Practice market sizing problems weekly
M Common Mistakes
  • ❌ Spending too long reading, too little analyzing
  • ❌ Not using data provided in the case
  • ❌ Recommendation without financial quantification
  • ❌ One person dominates the presentation
  • ❌ Slides with walls of text
  • ❌ No implementation plan or risk assessment

✓ All members contribute: In both prep and presentation
