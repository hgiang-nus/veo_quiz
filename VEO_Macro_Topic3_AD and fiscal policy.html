THE KEYNESIAN INSIGHT

Why Aggregate
Demand Matters

John Maynard Keynes & the Great Depression

Before Keynes, classical economists believed markets always self-correct. Then came the Great Depression (1929–1939).

The Vicious Cycle
Spending falls
→
Firms cut production
→
Workers laid off
→
Income falls
→
Spending falls further
📘 Keynes' Solution
When private spending collapses, the government must step in and spend to break the cycle. This is the foundation of fiscal policy.

What is Aggregate Demand?

Aggregate Demand (AD) = the total spending on goods and services in the economy at each price level.

AD = C + I + G + (X − M)

"Aggregate" means total / added up. AD is the macroeconomic version of demand — it's not about one good, but everything the economy produces.

The 4 Components of AD

🏠

C — Consumption

Household spending on goods & services
~65% of GDP

🏭

I — Investment

Business spending on capital + residential construction + inventories
~20% of GDP

🏛️

G — Government

Government purchases of goods & services (NOT transfers)
~15% of GDP

🌍

NX — Net Exports

Exports minus Imports
Can be + (surplus) or − (deficit)

Consumption (C) — The Largest Component

📘 The Consumption Function (Keynes)
$$C = C_0 + MPC \times Y_d$$
  • $C_0$ = autonomous consumption (spending that occurs even at zero income — necessities)
  • $MPC$ = marginal propensity to consume = fraction of each additional dollar of income that is spent
  • $Y_d$ = disposable income (income after taxes)

MPC — Marginal Propensity to Consume

$$MPC = \frac{\Delta C}{\Delta Y_d}$$

If MPC = 0.8: of every extra $1 of income, $0.80 is spent and $0.20 is saved.

MPS — Marginal Propensity to Save

$$MPS = 1 - MPC$$

If MPC = 0.8, then MPS = 0.2.

Key: MPC + MPS = 1 always.

📊 MPC Example

Suppose $C_0 = 100$ and $MPC = 0.75$. Income (Yd) = 1,000.

$$C = 100 + 0.75 \times 1{,}000 = 100 + 750 = 850$$

If income rises to 1,200:

$$C = 100 + 0.75 \times 1{,}200 = 100 + 900 = 1{,}000$$

Change in C = 1,000 − 850 = 150. Change in Yd = 200. MPC = 150/200 = 0.75 ✓

💡 Why MPC matters for fiscal policy
The higher the MPC, the more each dollar of government spending or tax cut gets re-spent in the economy → the bigger the multiplier effect.

THE AD CURVE

Why Does AD
Slope Downward?

The AD Curve

The AD curve shows the total quantity of output demanded at each price level.

It slopes downward: as the price level rises, the total quantity of goods and services demanded falls.

⚠️ NOT the same as micro demand
Micro: higher price of one good → substitute to another.
Macro: higher overall price level → can't substitute to "another economy." The reasons are different!

3 Reasons AD Slopes Downward

💰

1. Wealth Effect
(Pigou Effect)

Higher price level → real value of money falls → people feel poorer → C ↓

📈

2. Interest Rate Effect
(Keynes Effect)

Higher price level → people need more money for transactions → interest rates ↑ → I ↓

🌍

3. Exchange Rate Effect
(Mundell-Fleming)

Higher price level → domestic goods more expensive relative to foreign → NX ↓

📝 Exam Tip
All three effects mean: ↑ Price Level → ↓ Quantity of output demanded → downward-sloping AD.

Shifts in the AD Curve

AD shifts when any component (C, I, G, NX) changes at a given price level.

AD shifts RIGHT (increase) ↗

  • Consumer confidence rises → C↑
  • Interest rates fall → I↑
  • Government spending rises → G↑
  • Tax cuts → Yd↑ → C↑
  • Currency depreciates → exports cheaper → NX↑
  • Foreign income rises → X↑
  • Money supply increases → r↓ → I↑

AD shifts LEFT (decrease) ↙

  • Consumer confidence falls → C↓
  • Interest rates rise → I↓
  • Government spending cuts → G↓
  • Tax increases → Yd↓ → C↓
  • Currency appreciates → NX↓
  • Foreign recession → X↓
  • Money supply contracts → r↑ → I↓

Visualising AD Shifts

Result of rightward AD shift:

  • Output (Y) increases
  • Price level rises (in short run)
  • Employment increases

Result of leftward AD shift:

  • Output (Y) falls
  • Price level drops
  • Unemployment rises

THE OTHER HALF

Aggregate Supply

Short-Run vs Long-Run

Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)

📘 Definition
The SRAS curve shows the total quantity of output firms are willing to produce at each price level, in the short run (when some input prices, especially wages, are sticky).

SRAS slopes upward: higher price level → higher profit margins (since wages are fixed) → firms produce more.

What shifts SRAS?

SRAS shifts RIGHT (↗) when:

  • Input prices fall (oil, materials)
  • Productivity improves
  • Favourable supply shocks
  • Lower business taxes

SRAS shifts LEFT (↙) when:

  • Input prices rise (oil crisis)
  • Natural disasters
  • Higher wages
  • Supply chain disruptions

Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)

📘 Definition
In the long run, all prices (including wages) are flexible. Output is determined by real factors: technology, capital, labour, natural resources. The LRAS is vertical at potential output $Y^*$ (full employment GDP).

Key implications:

  • In the long run, output = Y* regardless of price level
  • Changes in AD only affect prices in the long run, not output
  • LRAS shifts right when potential output grows (more capital, labour, technology)

Classical Dichotomy

In the long run, real variables (output, employment) are determined by supply-side factors. Money and demand only affect nominal variables (price level).

AD-AS Equilibrium

📘 Short-Run Equilibrium
Where AD intersects SRAS. This determines the short-run price level and output. Output can be above, below, or at potential (Y*).

Y = Y*

At full employment
No output gap

Y < Y*

Recessionary gap
Unemployment above natural rate
Need expansionary policy

Y > Y*

Inflationary gap
Economy overheating
Need contractionary policy

Demand Shocks & Supply Shocks

Positive Demand Shock

AD shifts right → Y↑, P↑

Examples: Consumer confidence surge, government stimulus, money supply expansion

Short run: Output and prices rise
Long run: Only prices rise (output returns to Y*)

Negative Supply Shock

SRAS shifts left → Y↓, P↑ (stagflation!)

Examples: Oil price spike, pandemic, natural disaster, supply chain crisis

This is the worst scenario: output falls AND prices rise. Policy dilemma — stimulating AD worsens inflation.

🇻🇳 Vietnam 2022
Global energy/food price shocks (Russia-Ukraine war) = negative supply shock → SRAS shifted left → cost-push inflation with growth pressure downward.

GOVERNMENT INTERVENTION

Fiscal Policy

Taxation and government spending to manage AD

What is Fiscal Policy?

📘 Definition
Fiscal policy = the use of government spending (G) and taxation (T) to influence aggregate demand and the overall economy.
📈

Expansionary

↑G and/or ↓T

Goal: stimulate the economy during recession

AD shifts right

📉

Contractionary

↓G and/or ↑T

Goal: cool down an overheating economy

AD shifts left

The Multiplier Effect

📘 Key Insight
A $1 increase in government spending leads to more than $1 increase in GDP. Why? Because spending becomes income for someone, who then spends part of it, which becomes income for someone else, and so on.
Gov't spends $100
→
Worker earns $100,
spends $80 (MPC=0.8)
→
Shop owner earns $80,
spends $64
→
Supplier earns $64,
spends $51.20
→
... and so on

Total = $100 + $80 + $64 + $51.20 + ... = $100 × $\frac{1}{1-0.8}$ = $100 × 5 = $500

The Government Spending Multiplier

$k_G = \dfrac{1}{1 - MPC} = \dfrac{1}{MPS}$

Change in GDP: $\Delta Y = k_G \times \Delta G$

📊 Example

MPC = 0.8. Government increases spending by $10 billion.

$k_G = \frac{1}{1-0.8} = \frac{1}{0.2} = 5$

$\Delta Y = 5 \times 10 = $ $50 billion

The Tax Multiplier

$k_T = -\dfrac{MPC}{1 - MPC}$

$\Delta Y = k_T \times \Delta T$

📊 Example

MPC = 0.8. Government cuts taxes by $10 billion ($\Delta T = -10$).

$k_T = -\frac{0.8}{0.2} = -4$

$\Delta Y = -4 \times (-10) = $ +$40 billion

💡 Key: Spending multiplier > Tax multiplier
$k_G = 5$ but $|k_T| = 4$. Why? A $1 tax cut → only $MPC of it is spent (rest is saved). A $1 of government spending → all $1 enters the spending stream immediately. Government spending is "more bang for the buck."

The Balanced Budget Multiplier

📘 What if G and T increase by the same amount?

Suppose the government increases spending by $\Delta G = 10$ AND increases taxes by $\Delta T = 10$ (balanced budget — no deficit change).

Effect of ΔG = 10: $\Delta Y = 5 \times 10 = +50$

Effect of ΔT = 10: $\Delta Y = -4 \times 10 = -40$

Net effect: $\Delta Y = +50 - 40 = $ +10

Balanced Budget Multiplier = 1

An equal increase in G and T always raises GDP by exactly the amount of the increase. Always = 1, regardless of MPC!

🏆 Practice: Multiplier Calculations

An economy has MPC = 0.75. The government wants to close a recessionary gap of $200 billion.

Q1: By how much should G increase?
Q2: Alternatively, by how much should T decrease?

Solution:

$k_G = \frac{1}{1-0.75} = 4$. Need $\Delta Y = 200$.

Q1: $\Delta G = \frac{200}{4} = $ $50 billion

$|k_T| = \frac{0.75}{0.25} = 3$. Need $\Delta Y = 200$.

Q2: $\Delta T = -\frac{200}{3} = $ −$66.67 billion (cut taxes by $66.67B)

Note: A larger tax cut is needed because the tax multiplier is smaller than the spending multiplier.

Automatic Stabilisers

📘 Definition
Automatic stabilisers are fiscal mechanisms that automatically adjust government spending or tax revenue without new legislation — they kick in as the economy changes.

During Recession (Y↓)

  • Tax revenue falls automatically (lower income → lower tax)
  • Unemployment benefits rise automatically (more people qualify)
  • Both inject spending into the economy → partially offset the downturn

During Boom (Y↑)

  • Tax revenue rises automatically (higher income → higher tax brackets)
  • Unemployment benefits fall (fewer people qualifying)
  • Both withdraw spending → partially cool the boom
Advantage: No legislative delay. Disadvantage: Often not strong enough alone — may need active ("discretionary") policy too.

The Crowding-Out Effect

⚠️ A Limitation of Fiscal Policy
When the government borrows to fund increased spending, it competes with private borrowers for loanable funds → interest rates rise → private investment falls.
G↑
→
Gov't borrows more
→
Demand for loanable funds↑
→
Interest rates↑
→
Private I↓

Partial Crowding Out

G↑ by $100 but I↓ by $30 → net ΔAD = $70. Multiplier effect is reduced but still positive.

Complete Crowding Out

G↑ by $100 but I↓ by $100 → net ΔAD = $0. Fiscal policy is ineffective. (Extreme case.)

Limitations of Fiscal Policy

LimitationDescription
Recognition lagTakes time to realise the economy is in recession (data is published with delay)
Decision lagPolitical process — parliament must debate and approve spending/tax changes
Implementation lagBuilding infrastructure takes years; tax changes take time to affect behaviour
Crowding outGovernment borrowing raises interest rates → reduces private investment
Government debtPersistent deficits → rising national debt → future tax burden
Political constraintsPoliticians may time policy for elections, not economic need
💡 Implication
Because of these lags, fiscal policy is better for severe, prolonged recessions. For mild, short-term fluctuations, monetary policy (Topic 4) is usually faster and more flexible.

Government Budget: Deficit & Debt

Budget Balance = Tax Revenue (T) − Government Spending (G)

Surplus

T > G
Government saves

Balanced

T = G
No borrowing

Deficit

T < G
Government borrows

📘 Deficit vs Debt
  • Deficit = flow (this year's shortfall). Like your monthly overspending.
  • Debt = stock (accumulated total owed). Like your total credit card balance.
  • Persistent deficits → growing debt → higher interest payments → less room for future spending.

🇻🇳 Vietnam's Fiscal Policy in Practice

COVID-19 Response (2020–2021)
  • Tax relief: Deferred corporate income tax, VAT reductions, land rent deferrals
  • Spending: Cash transfers to affected workers; National Vaccine Fund
  • Fiscal discipline: Vietnam maintained relatively low public debt (~44% of GDP) — providing room for stimulus
2022–2023: Recovery & Infrastructure
  • Infrastructure push: Massive public investment in highways, airports (Long Thanh), metro systems
  • VAT cut: Temporary reduction from 10% to 8% to stimulate consumer spending
  • Budget deficit target: ~3–4% of GDP

🏆 Practice: Multiplier Deep Dive

In an economy with MPC = 0.6:

(a) What is the spending multiplier?
(b) Government increases G by $20B. What is ΔY?
(c) Government cuts taxes by $20B. What is ΔY?
(d) Government increases G by $20B AND T by $20B. What is ΔY?

Solutions:

(a) $k_G = \frac{1}{1-0.6} = \frac{1}{0.4} = $ 2.5

(b) $\Delta Y = 2.5 \times 20 = $ $50B

(c) $k_T = -\frac{0.6}{0.4} = -1.5$. $\Delta Y = -1.5 \times (-20) = $ $30B

(d) Balanced budget: $\Delta Y = 50 - 30 = $ $20B (multiplier = 1 ✓)

🏆 IEO-Style: Fiscal Policy Analysis

Vietnam's economy is experiencing a recessionary gap. The government has two options:

Option A: Increase government spending on infrastructure by 50 trillion VND.
Option B: Cut personal income taxes by 50 trillion VND.

Assuming MPC = 0.8, which option generates a larger increase in GDP? Explain.

Answer:

Option A: $k_G = 5$. $\Delta Y = 5 \times 50 = $ 250 trillion VND

Option B: $|k_T| = 4$. $\Delta Y = 4 \times 50 = $ 200 trillion VND

Option A (spending) is more effective because every dollar of G enters the spending stream immediately, while part of a tax cut is saved (MPS × ΔT doesn't get spent).

The Keynesian Cross (Advanced)

📘 Model

Planned expenditure: $PE = C + I + G + NX$

Equilibrium: $Y = PE$ (output = planned spending)

$PE = C_0 + MPC(Y - T) + I + G + NX$

Equilibrium output:

$$Y^* = \frac{1}{1-MPC}(C_0 - MPC \cdot T + I + G + NX)$$

The $\frac{1}{1-MPC}$ is the multiplier — it amplifies any change in autonomous spending.

Open Economy Multiplier (Advanced)

In an open economy with imports, the multiplier is smaller because some spending leaks abroad:

$$k = \frac{1}{1 - MPC + MPM}$$

where $MPM$ = marginal propensity to import (fraction of extra income spent on imports).

📊 Example

MPC = 0.8, MPM = 0.2

Closed economy multiplier: $\frac{1}{1-0.8} = 5$

Open economy multiplier: $\frac{1}{1-0.8+0.2} = \frac{1}{0.4} = 2.5$

The multiplier is halved because spending leaks to imports. Very relevant for Vietnam (trade/GDP ≈ 200%).

Multiplier with Proportional Tax (Advanced)

With a proportional tax rate $t$ (e.g., 20%):

$$k = \frac{1}{1 - MPC(1-t)}$$
📊 Example

MPC = 0.8, tax rate t = 0.25

$k = \frac{1}{1 - 0.8(1-0.25)} = \frac{1}{1 - 0.8 \times 0.75} = \frac{1}{1 - 0.6} = \frac{1}{0.4} = 2.5$

Without tax: $k = 5$. With 25% tax: $k = 2.5$. Taxes act as an automatic stabiliser — they reduce the multiplier and dampen economic fluctuations.

The more leakages (saving, taxes, imports), the smaller the multiplier. Real-world multipliers are typically 1–2, not the theoretical 5.

⚠️ Common Exam Mistakes

❌ Don't
  • Confuse movement along AD (price change) with shift of AD
  • Forget that G excludes transfer payments
  • Mix up spending multiplier and tax multiplier
  • Ignore crowding out when evaluating fiscal policy
  • Say "fiscal policy = printing money" (that's monetary)
  • Forget: LRAS is vertical, SRAS is upward-sloping
✅ Do
  • Always state which curve shifts and in which direction
  • Show multiplier calculations step by step
  • Distinguish short-run (AD matters) vs long-run (AS matters)
  • Mention time lags and crowding out as limitations
