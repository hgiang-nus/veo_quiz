The AS-AD Model
Aggregate Demand, Short-Run Aggregate Supply, Long-Run Aggregate Supply — how the macroeconomy reaches equilibrium and responds to shocks.
Part 1
The Big Picture
The AS-AD model is the workhorse of macroeconomics — it explains how the overall price level and real GDP are determined simultaneously.
Overview
What is the AS-AD Model?

The Macro Framework

The AS-AD model shows how the aggregate price level (P) and real GDP (Y) are determined by the interaction of aggregate demand and aggregate supply.

Three Curves

  • AD — Aggregate Demand (downward-sloping)
  • SRAS — Short-Run Aggregate Supply (upward-sloping)
  • LRAS — Long-Run Aggregate Supply (vertical)

Key Variables

Y-axis: General Price Level (P)
X-axis: Real GDP (Y)
Yf: Potential GDP (full employment output)

Part 2
Aggregate Demand
Why the AD curve slopes downward, what shifts it, and how to analyze demand-side changes.
Aggregate Demand
The AD Curve

What is AD?

AD shows the total quantity of goods and services demanded in the economy at each price level.

AD = C + I + G + (X − M)

Consumption + Investment + Government spending + Net exports

AD Slopes Downward — Why?

As P ↓ → total quantity demanded ↑. Three effects explain this:

Aggregate Demand
Why AD Slopes Downward — Three Effects

1. Wealth Effect

P↓ → real value of money holdings ↑ → consumers feel wealthier → C↑ → Y↑

Also called the Pigou Effect

2. Interest Rate Effect

P↓ → need less money for transactions → interest rates ↓ → borrowing cheaper → I↑ → Y↑

Also called the Keynes Effect

3. Exchange Rate Effect

P↓ → domestic goods cheaper relative to foreign → exports↑, imports↓ → NX↑ → Y↑

Also called the Mundell-Fleming Effect

Vietnam 2023: When inflation cooled, consumers' purchasing power rose (wealth effect), the State Bank kept interest rates low (interest rate effect), and Vietnamese exports became more competitive (exchange rate effect) — all boosting aggregate demand.

Aggregate Demand
AD Shifters — What Shifts the AD Curve?
C

Consumer Spending (C)

Consumer confidence, wealth, taxes on households, expectations

I

Investment (I)

Business confidence, interest rates, corporate taxes, technology

G

Government Spending (G)

Fiscal policy — government increases/decreases its expenditure

NX

Net Exports (X−M)

Exchange rates, foreign income, trade policies, tariffs

Money Supply

Central bank ↑ money supply → interest rates ↓ → I↑, C↑ → AD shifts right.

Vietnam's fiscal stimulus 2022: Gov't raised public investment (G↑) and extended tax breaks → AD shifted right → GDP growth rebounded to 8%.

Part 3
Short-Run Aggregate Supply
Why SRAS slopes upward, what causes it to shift, and how it differs from LRAS.
SRAS
The SRAS Curve & Why It Slopes Upward

What is SRAS?

SRAS shows the total quantity of goods and services firms are willing to supply at each price level in the short run (when some input prices, especially wages, are sticky/fixed).

Why SRAS Slopes Upward

When P ↑ but nominal wages are sticky:

  • Sticky-Wage Theory: Wages fixed by contracts → real wage falls → hiring cheaper → firms produce more
  • Sticky-Price Theory: Some firms slow to adjust prices → relatively cheaper → sell more
  • Misperception Theory: Firms mistake general P↑ for higher relative prices → increase output

Key Assumption

In the short run, at least some input prices (especially nominal wages) are fixed. This is why the SRAS is not vertical — firms respond to price changes by adjusting output.

SRAS
SRAS Shifters — What Shifts SRAS?
💰

Input Prices (Wages, Raw Materials)

Wages↑ or oil prices↑ → costs↑ → SRAS shifts left

⚡

Productivity / Technology

Better tech → produce more at same cost → SRAS shifts right

📜

Government Regulation & Taxes

Higher business taxes / regulation → costs↑ → SRAS shifts left. Subsidies → SRAS right

🌍

Supply Shocks

Natural disasters, pandemics, wars → sudden cost changes → SRAS shifts

Expected Price Level

If workers expect higher prices → demand higher wages → costs↑ → SRAS shifts left. This is the key to long-run adjustment!

Oil shock 2022: Russia-Ukraine war → global oil prices surged → production costs rose across Vietnam → SRAS shifted left → inflation + slower growth.

Part 4
Long-Run Aggregate Supply
Why LRAS is vertical, what determines potential GDP, and what shifts it over time.
LRAS
The LRAS Curve — Vertical at Yf

Why Is LRAS Vertical?

In the long run, all prices and wages are fully flexible. Output depends only on the economy's real resources — labour, capital, technology, natural resources — not the price level.

No matter what P is, the economy produces at its potential GDP (Yf) — the full-employment level of output.

What Determines Yf?

  • Labour force size and quality
  • Capital stock (machines, infrastructure)
  • Technology and productivity
  • Natural resources
  • Institutions (property rights, rule of law)

Vietnam's potential GDP grows as the labour force expands, FDI builds factories (Samsung, Intel plants in Bắc Ninh), and workers gain skills — shifting LRAS right over time.

LRAS
LRAS Shifts = Economic Growth

When LRAS Shifts Right

The economy's productive capacity expands — potential GDP increases. This is long-run economic growth.

  • More/better labour (education, immigration)
  • More capital (investment, infrastructure)
  • Technological progress
  • Discovery of natural resources
  • Institutional improvement

LRAS Shifts Left (Rare)

War destruction, natural catastrophe, brain drain, institutional collapse → productive capacity shrinks.

Vietnam 2000–2025: Massive FDI (Samsung, LG, Intel), rising education levels, infrastructure investment (expressways, ports) → LRAS shifted right dramatically → GDP per capita rose from ~$400 to over $4,000.

The Critical Link

In the short run, the economy can produce above or below Yf. In the long run, wage/price adjustments bring output back to Yf. The SRAS shifts until the economy returns to LRAS — this is self-correction.

Part 5
Equilibrium & Adjustment
Short-run equilibrium, long-run equilibrium, output gaps, and the economy's self-correcting mechanism.
Equilibrium
Short-Run vs. Long-Run Equilibrium

Short-Run Equilibrium

Where AD intersects SRAS. Determines actual P and Y. Output can differ from Yf.

Long-Run Equilibrium

Where AD, SRAS, and LRAS all intersect. The economy is at Yf — full employment, no output gap.

Two Types of Gaps

Recessionary gap: Y < Yf (unemployment)
Inflationary gap: Y > Yf (overheating)

LR Yf ELR All three curves meet at one point
Output Gaps
Inflationary Gap (AD Increase)

What Happens

AD shifts right → SR equilibrium moves to E₁ where Y₁ > Yf. The economy is overheating — producing beyond its capacity.

Self-Correction (Long Run)

Y > Yf → tight labour market → workers demand higher wages → costs rise → SRAS shifts left → economy returns to Yf at a higher price level P₂.

Vietnam 2022: Post-COVID stimulus + pent-up demand → economy grew 8% (above potential) → inflation rose to 4.5% → State Bank tightened policy.

Output Gaps
Recessionary Gap (AD Decrease)

What Happens

AD shifts left → SR equilibrium at E₁ where Y₁ < Yf. The economy is in recession — high unemployment, output below potential.

Self-Correction (Long Run)

Y < Yf → high unemployment → workers accept lower wages → costs fall → SRAS shifts right → economy returns to Yf at a lower price level P₂.

⚠️ Keynesian critique: this can take very long. "In the long run, we are all dead."

Vietnam COVID-19 (2020-21): Lockdowns → C↓, I↓, NX↓ → AD shifted left → GDP growth slowed to 2.9% (well below 7% potential) → recessionary gap. Gov't responded with fiscal stimulus rather than waiting for self-correction.

Supply Shocks
Negative Supply Shock → Stagflation

Stagflation = Stagnation + Inflation

SRAS shifts left (costs↑) → the economy experiences the worst of both worlds:

  • Price level ↑ (inflation)
  • Real GDP ↓ (recession)
  • Unemployment ↑

Policy Dilemma

Expand AD? Fixes recession but worsens inflation.
Contract AD? Fights inflation but deepens recession.
No easy solution — the gov't must choose which problem to prioritize.

1970s Oil Crisis: OPEC quadrupled oil prices → SRAS shifted left globally → stagflation. 2022: Similar dynamics as energy costs surged after the Russia-Ukraine conflict.

Supply Shocks
Positive Supply Shock (SRAS Shifts Right)

The Best-Case Scenario

Technology breakthrough, input prices fall, or productivity surge → SRAS shifts right:

  • Price level ↓ (disinflation)
  • Real GDP ↑
  • Unemployment ↓

The opposite of stagflation — growth with falling prices!

US 1990s tech boom: IT revolution → huge productivity gains → SRAS shifted right → rapid growth with low inflation. Vietnam: FDI inflows + tech adoption shifted SRAS right, enabling ~7% growth with moderate inflation.

Part 6
Policy Responses
How governments use fiscal and monetary policy to close output gaps, and the Classical vs Keynesian debate.
Policy
Fiscal Policy in the AS-AD Model

What is Fiscal Policy?

Government adjusts G (spending) and T (taxes) to shift AD.

Expansionary

G↑ or T↓ → AD shifts right → closes recessionary gap

Contractionary

G↓ or T↑ → AD shifts left → closes inflationary gap

Multiplier Effect

Initial G↑ → income↑ → spending↑ → more income → AD shifts by more than the initial change in G.

Vietnam 2020-21: Gov't cut VAT (T↓), raised infrastructure spending (G↑) → AD shifted right → helped close the recessionary gap from COVID.

Policy
Monetary Policy in the AS-AD Model

What is Monetary Policy?

Central bank adjusts money supply and interest rates to shift AD.

Expansionary (Easy)

Money supply↑ → interest rate↓ → I↑, C↑ → AD right

Buy bonds, cut rates

Contractionary (Tight)

Money supply↓ → interest rate↑ → I↓, C↓ → AD left

Sell bonds, raise rates

State Bank of Vietnam (SBV): In 2023, cut policy rates 4 times (from 6% to 4.5%) → cheaper borrowing → C↑, I↑ → AD shifted right → supported recovery. In 2022, SBV raised rates to fight inflation (contractionary).

Supply-Side Policies

Some policies shift SRAS or LRAS directly: deregulation, education investment, infrastructure, tax reform, R&D subsidies. These boost productive capacity rather than just demand.

Debate
Classical vs. Keynesian Views

Classical View

  • Prices and wages are flexible
  • Economy self-corrects quickly
  • SRAS is nearly vertical (close to LRAS)
  • Government intervention is unnecessary — markets adjust
  • AD shifts mainly affect prices, not output

"Leave the market alone"

Keynesian View

  • Prices and wages are sticky (especially downward)
  • Self-correction is slow and painful
  • SRAS is relatively flat
  • Government should intervene (fiscal/monetary policy)
  • AD shifts mainly affect output and employment

"In the long run, we are all dead" — Keynes

Most real economies (including Vietnam) take a pragmatic approach: use fiscal & monetary policy in recessions (Keynesian), but also invest in supply-side reforms (Classical) for long-run growth.

Summary
Self-Correction Mechanism

Inflationary Gap (Y > Yf)

① AD shifts right → Y > Yf
② Tight labour market → wages↑
③ Costs↑ → SRAS shifts left
④ Y returns to Yf at higher P

Recessionary Gap (Y < Yf)

① AD shifts left → Y < Yf
② High unemployment → wages↓
③ Costs↓ → SRAS shifts right
④ Y returns to Yf at lower P

Why Policy Matters

Self-correction works through wage adjustment — but wages are sticky downward (workers resist pay cuts). Recessionary gaps can persist for years → Keynesian argument for active policy (fiscal expansion or monetary easing) to close the gap faster.

Great Depression (1930s): The economy did NOT self-correct quickly — unemployment lasted a decade. This led Keynes to argue for government intervention. 2008-09 GFC: Governments worldwide used massive fiscal and monetary stimulus rather than waiting for self-correction.

How to Answer AS-AD Questions (5 Steps)

Step-by-Step Method

  • Step 1: Draw the initial long-run equilibrium (AD, SRAS, LRAS all intersecting at E₀, Yf, P₀)
  • Step 2: Identify the shock — is it a demand-side or supply-side change?
  • Step 3: Shift the correct curve (AD or SRAS) in the correct direction
  • Step 4: Find the new short-run equilibrium (E₁) — state what happens to P and Y
  • Step 5: Explain the long-run adjustment (SRAS shifts via wage adjustment) → new LR equilibrium at Yf

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing movement along AD with AD shift
  • Shifting LRAS for short-run shocks
  • Forgetting LRAS is vertical (not sloped)
  • Not showing the self-correction step
  • Moving SRAS for demand-side shocks

Diagram Checklist

  • ✓ Label axes: P (y-axis), Y (x-axis)
  • ✓ Draw all three curves (AD, SRAS, LRAS)
  • ✓ Mark Yf on x-axis
  • ✓ Label E₀, E₁, (E₂ for LR)
  • ✓ Show direction of shift with arrows
  • ✓ State P and Y effects explicitly

Practice Questions

Q1: Demand Shock

"The government increases public spending on infrastructure. Using an AS-AD diagram, explain the short-run and long-run effects on the price level and real GDP."

Hint: G↑ → AD shifts right → SR: P↑, Y↑ (above Yf) → LR: wages↑ → SRAS shifts left → P↑↑, Y returns to Yf

Q2: Supply Shock

"A sharp rise in global oil prices hits Vietnam's economy. Explain the short-run impact using the AS-AD model. What is this phenomenon called?"

Hint: Oil P↑ → costs↑ → SRAS shifts left → P↑ and Y↓ simultaneously = stagflation

Q3: Policy Response

"The economy is in a recessionary gap. Compare the Keynesian and Classical approaches to closing the gap."

Hint: Keynesian → fiscal/monetary expansion (shift AD right now). Classical → wait for wages to fall (SRAS shifts right eventually). Discuss speed, side effects, and real-world practicality.

Q4: Long-Run Growth

"Vietnam's GDP has grown rapidly over the past two decades. Explain this using the LRAS concept. What factors shifted LRAS to the right?"

Hint: FDI (capital↑), rising education (labour quality↑), technology adoption, infrastructure → LRAS shifts right → Yf increases.
