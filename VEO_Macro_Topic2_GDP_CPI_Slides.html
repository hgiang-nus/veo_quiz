PART 1

Gross Domestic
Product (GDP)

The single most important measure of an economy's performance

What is GDP?

GDP = the market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period of time.

Let's unpack each part of this definition:

"Market value" — uses prices to add different goods together (you can't just add 3 cars + 100 apples)
"Final goods" — only goods sold to end users, NOT intermediate goods (to avoid double-counting)
"Within a country" — by location of production, regardless of producer's nationality
"Given period" — usually a year or quarter; it's a flow measure, not a stock

Rule 1: Use Market Prices

📘 Rule
To compute total value of different goods, use their market prices as weights.
📊 Example: Apple & Orange Economy

Suppose: Apples cost $0.50 each, Oranges cost $1.00 each. We produce 4 apples and 3 oranges.

GDP = ($0.50 × 4) + ($1.00 × 3) = $2.00 + $3.00 = $5.00

💡 Why prices?
Prices reflect how much society values each good. A $50,000 car contributes more to GDP than a $2 pencil because society values it more.

Rule 2: Only New Production

📘 Rule
Used goods are NOT included in GDP. Only goods produced in the current period count.

✅ Counted in GDP

  • A brand-new Honda motorbike sold at a dealership
  • A freshly built house
  • This year's rice harvest

❌ NOT counted

  • A second-hand motorbike sold on Facebook
  • A resale of an existing house
  • Last year's unsold inventory (already counted)
Exception: The service of the real estate agent who facilitates a resale IS counted (it's a new service produced this year).

Rule 3: Inventories

📘 Rule
Goods produced but not yet sold are counted as inventory investment in GDP.

Example: Bicycle Company

A bicycle produced in November 2022 and delivered to a shop, but not sold until March 2023:

  • 2022 GDP: Counted as inventory investment
  • 2023 GDP: When sold, inventory ↓ (disinvestment) and consumption ↑ — net effect on 2023 GDP = 0

Spoilage

If goods spoil before being sold:

  • Initially counted as inventory investment
  • When spoiled, inventory decreases
  • GDP unchanged (production occurred, waste is just a loss)

🏆 VEO Practice: Inventories

In 1992, Trailblazer Bicycle Company produced a mountain bike, delivered to retail in Nov 1992. Sold to E.Z. Ryder in March 1993. This bicycle is counted as:

  • (a) Disinvestment in 1992 and consumption in 1993
  • (b) Disinvestment in 1992 and investment in 1993
  • (c) Investment in 1992 and disinvestment in 1993 ✓
  • (d) Consumption in 1992 and disinvestment in 1993
Produced 1992 → inventory investment (1992). Sold 1993 → inventory drops (disinvestment) + consumption rises → net zero impact on 1993 GDP.

Rule 4: Final Goods Only (No Double-Counting)

📘 Rule
Only final goods are counted. Intermediate goods (inputs used to produce other goods) are excluded because their value is already embedded in the final good's price.
📊 Example: A Loaf of Bread
StageProducerRevenueValue Added
WheatFarmer$1.00$1.00
FlourMiller$2.00$1.00
BreadBaker$3.50$1.50
RetailShop$4.00$0.50
Total$10.50 (wrong!)$4.00 ✓

GDP counts only the final price ($4.00) OR the sum of value added ($4.00). Never sum all revenues ($10.50) — that double-counts!

Value Added

Value Added = Revenue − Cost of Intermediate Inputs

Value added is the contribution of each firm to total output. Summing value added across all firms = GDP.

📊 VEO 2022 Example

1,500 food companies, each with: Revenue = 300 (thousand VND), Costs = 150 (thousand VND)

Value Added per firm = 300 − 150 = 150 (thousand VND)

GDP = 1,500 × 150 × 1,000 = 225,000,000 VND

Rule 5: Imputed Values

📘 Rule
Some goods/services are not sold in markets and don't have prices. We use imputed (estimated) values.

✅ Included (with imputed values)

  • Owner-occupied housing: Estimated rental value
  • Government services: Valued at cost of providing them
  • Food grown and consumed by farmers: Sometimes estimated

❌ NOT included

  • Unpaid housework (cooking, cleaning)
  • Illegal activities (drug trade)
  • Underground economy (unreported income)

Why? Too hard to measure reliably.

THREE EQUIVALENT APPROACHES

Methods of
Computing GDP

Three Methods of Computing GDP

All three give the same answer — they're different views of the same thing.

💰

1. Expenditure

Total spending on final goods & services

Y = C + I + G + NX

👷

2. Income

Total income earned by factors of production

Y = W + R + i + π + D + Tₑ

🏭

3. Output

Total value added across all industries

Y = Σ Value Added

Method 1: Expenditure Approach

Y = C + I + G + NX

ComponentWhat it includes% of GDP (typical)
C (Consumption)Household spending on goods & services (food, housing, transport, entertainment)~60–70%
I (Investment)Business spending on capital goods + residential construction + inventory changes~20–25%
G (Gov't Purchases)Government spending on goods & services (NOT transfers like pensions)~15–20%
NX (Net Exports)Exports − Imports. Can be positive (surplus) or negative (deficit)Varies

Consumption (C)

📘 Definition
Spending by households on goods and services. The largest component of GDP.

Three categories:

Durable Goods

Last a long time:
cars, furniture, appliances

Non-durable Goods

Used up quickly:
food, clothing, fuel

Services

Intangible:
healthcare, education, haircuts

⚠️ NOT consumption
Purchase of a new house = Investment (I), not consumption. Purchase of stocks/bonds = not GDP at all (financial transaction, not production).

Investment (I)

📘 Definition
Spending on goods that will be used to produce more goods in the future. Also called Gross Private Domestic Investment.

Three categories:

Business Fixed

Factories, machinery, equipment, computers

Residential

New houses and apartments

Inventory

Changes in firms' stocks of unsold goods

⚠️ Common confusion
In economics, "investment" ≠ buying stocks. Investment = spending on new physical capital or inventory. Buying stocks is a financial transaction — it doesn't produce new goods.

Government Purchases (G) & Net Exports (NX)

🏛️ Government Purchases (G)

Spending by government on goods and services: public schools, roads, military, government employees' salaries.

NOT included in G: Transfer payments (pensions, unemployment benefits, welfare). These are redistributions, not purchases of goods/services.
🌍 Net Exports (NX)

$NX = \text{Exports} - \text{Imports}$

  • Exports (+): Foreign spending on domestic goods → adds to GDP
  • Imports (−): Domestic spending on foreign goods → subtracted from GDP

If NX > 0: Trade Surplus
If NX < 0: Trade Deficit

Method 2: Income Approach

GDP = W + R + i + π + D + Tₑ

SymbolComponentDescription
WWagesCompensation of employees (largest component)
RRentIncome from land and property
iInterestIncome from lending capital
πProfitCorporate profits (distributed + retained)
DDepreciationConsumption of fixed capital (wear and tear)
TₑIndirect taxesVAT, excise taxes (included in market prices)
Logic: Every dollar spent (expenditure) becomes income for someone → Income ≡ Expenditure.

Method 3: Output (Value Added) Approach

GDP = Σ (Value Added across all industries)

Value Added = Revenue − Cost of Intermediate Inputs

📊 Example: Vietnam's GDP by Sector (2023)
SectorShare of GDPExamples
Agriculture, Forestry, Fishery~12%Rice, seafood, coffee, rubber
Industry & Construction~38%Electronics (Samsung), textiles, construction
Services~42%Trade, tourism, finance, telecom
Product taxes minus subsidies~8%VAT, excise taxes

🏆 VEO 2021: Real GDP

If real GDP has increased, but the production of goods has remained the same, then the production of services has:

  • (a) Increased ✓
  • (b) Decreased
  • (c) Remained the same
  • (d) Fluctuated
Real GDP = goods + services. If goods output is unchanged but real GDP rose, then services output must have increased to make up the difference.

CRITICAL DISTINCTION

Nominal vs Real GDP

Separating price changes from quantity changes

Nominal vs Real GDP

💵 Nominal GDP

Value of output at current prices.

Changes when either prices or quantities change.

Problem: If nominal GDP rises 10%, did we produce more? Or did prices just go up?

📏 Real GDP

Value of output at constant (base-year) prices.

Changes only when quantities change.

Solution: Real GDP removes the effect of inflation — it measures true production changes.

Real GDP is what we use to measure economic growth and compare across years.

📊 Computing Real GDP: Example

Apple & Orange economy. Base year = 2022.

2022 (Base Year)2023
PriceQuantityPriceQuantity
Apples$1100$1.50120
Oranges$250$2.5060

Nominal GDP 2023

= ($1.50 × 120) + ($2.50 × 60)
= $180 + $150 = $330

Real GDP 2023 (2022 prices)

= ($1 × 120) + ($2 × 60)
= $120 + $120 = $240

Nominal GDP ($330) overstates growth because it includes price increases. Real GDP ($240) shows true production growth.

The GDP Deflator

GDP Deflator = $\dfrac{\text{Nominal GDP}}{\text{Real GDP}} \times 100$

The GDP Deflator measures the overall price level relative to the base year. It tells us how much of nominal GDP growth is due to inflation vs real growth.

📊 Continuing our example

Real GDP 2022 = ($1 × 100) + ($2 × 50) = $200. Nominal GDP 2022 = $200 (base year → same).

Deflator 2022 = (200/200) × 100 = 100

Deflator 2023 = (330/240) × 100 = 137.5

Interpretation: Prices rose 37.5% from 2022 to 2023.

Useful Relationships

% Δ Nominal GDP ≈ % Δ Real GDP + % Δ GDP Deflator

Or equivalently:

% Δ Real GDP ≈ % Δ Nominal GDP − Inflation Rate

⚠️ This is an approximation
Works well when changes are small. For exact calculations, use: Real GDP = Nominal GDP / (Deflator/100).

🏆 VEO 2021: GDP Deflator Calculation

Nominal GDP: 3,421 → 3,521 USD. GDP Deflator: 262 → 270.
Find: %Δ Nominal GDP (A), %Δ Deflator (B), %Δ Real GDP (C).

Solution:

A: %Δ Nominal = (3,521 − 3,421) / 3,421 × 100 = 2.92%

B: %Δ Deflator = (270 − 262) / 262 × 100 = 3.05%

C: %Δ Real ≈ 2.92% − 3.05% = −0.13%

Answer: (a) 2.92, 3.05, negative 0.13

Key insight: Nominal GDP rose but real GDP fell — prices grew faster than output. The economy didn't truly grow!

RELATED CONCEPT

GDP vs GNP

GDP vs GNP

GNP = GDP + Net Income from Abroad

GDP 🏗️

Production within a country's borders, by anyone (domestic or foreign).

Includes: Samsung factory output in Vietnam
Excludes: Vietnamese worker's earnings in Japan

GNP 🏠

Production by a country's residents, anywhere in the world.

Includes: Vietnamese worker's earnings in Japan
Excludes: Samsung's profits repatriated to Korea

🇻🇳 Vietnam: GDP > GNP
Because Vietnam receives large FDI (Samsung, Intel, etc.), profits earned by foreign firms are part of Vietnam's GDP but NOT GNP. Net income from abroad is negative → GNP < GDP.

MEASURING PRICES

Consumer Price
Index (CPI)

Consumer Price Index (CPI)

📘 Definition
The CPI measures the cost of a fixed basket of goods and services typically purchased by consumers, relative to a base period.

$CPI_t = \dfrac{\sum P_t^i \cdot Q_0^i}{\sum P_0^i \cdot Q_0^i} \times 100$

where $P_t^i$ = price of good $i$ in period $t$, $Q_0^i$ = quantity in base period

Key: the quantities are fixed at base-year levels. Only prices change.

📊 CPI Calculation Example

A Vietnamese consumer's basket: phở and cà phê.

Base Year (2020) PriceBase Year Qty2023 Price
Phở30,000 VND200 bowls/yr40,000 VND
Cà phê20,000 VND300 cups/yr25,000 VND

Base year cost (2020)

= (30,000 × 200) + (20,000 × 300)
= 6,000,000 + 6,000,000
= 12,000,000 VND

CPI₂₀₂₀ = 100

2023 cost (same basket)

= (40,000 × 200) + (25,000 × 300)
= 8,000,000 + 7,500,000
= 15,500,000 VND

CPI₂₀₂₃ = (15.5M / 12M) × 100 = 129.2

Interpretation: prices rose 29.2% from 2020 to 2023.

Computing Inflation from CPI

Inflation Rate = $\dfrac{CPI_t - CPI_{t-1}}{CPI_{t-1}} \times 100\%$

📊 Example

CPI in 2022 = 125.0. CPI in 2023 = 129.2.

Inflation = (129.2 − 125.0) / 125.0 × 100 = 3.36%

🏆 VEO 2021
Q: We calculate CPI in order to:
Answer: (b) Monitor changes in the cost of living over time.

CPI vs GDP Deflator

FeatureCPIGDP Deflator
BasketFixed (base-year quantities)Changes (current-year quantities)
Goods coveredConsumer goods only (incl. imports)ALL domestically produced goods
Imports✅ Included (consumers buy imports)❌ Excluded (not domestic production)
Investment goods❌ Excluded✅ Included
Index typeLaspeyres (base-year weights)Paasche (current-year weights)
BiasOverstates inflation (ignores substitution)Understates inflation
⚠️ Substitution Bias in CPI
CPI uses a fixed basket. When prices change, consumers substitute toward cheaper goods. CPI misses this → overstates cost of living.

🏆 Practice: GDP Calculation

An economy produces only 3 goods:

Good2022 Price2022 Qty2023 Price2023 Qty
Rice$2500$3480
Coffee$5200$6220
Fish$4300$4350

Find: Nominal GDP (2023), Real GDP (2023, base=2022), GDP Deflator (2023), Inflation rate.

📊 Solution

Nominal GDP 2023

= ($3 × 480) + ($6 × 220) + ($4 × 350)
= 1,440 + 1,320 + 1,400
= $4,160

Real GDP 2023 (2022 prices)

= ($2 × 480) + ($5 × 220) + ($4 × 350)
= 960 + 1,100 + 1,400
= $3,460

GDP Deflator 2023

= (4,160 / 3,460) × 100
= 120.2

Real GDP 2022

= ($2×500) + ($5×200) + ($4×300)
= 1,000 + 1,000 + 1,200 = $3,200

Real GDP growth = (3,460 − 3,200) / 3,200 × 100 = 8.13%
Inflation (deflator) = (120.2 − 100) / 100 × 100 = 20.2%

🏆 Practice: CPI Calculation

Using the same data (base year = 2022), calculate CPI for 2023.

Solution

CPI uses base-year quantities with current prices:

Cost of 2022 basket at 2022 prices:
= ($2×500) + ($5×200) + ($4×300) = $3,200

Cost of 2022 basket at 2023 prices:
= ($3×500) + ($6×200) + ($4×300) = 1,500 + 1,200 + 1,200 = $3,900

CPI₂₀₂₃ = (3,900 / 3,200) × 100 = 121.9

CPI inflation = 21.9%

Note: CPI inflation (21.9%) ≠ GDP Deflator inflation (20.2%) — different baskets!

Limitations of GDP

GDP is useful but imperfect. It does NOT measure:

❌ Not captured by GDP

  • Income distribution: GDP can rise while inequality worsens
  • Leisure time: Working more hours raises GDP but reduces well-being
  • Environmental quality: Pollution isn't subtracted; cleanup is added
  • Non-market production: Housework, volunteer work, care for elderly
  • Underground economy: Unreported income, illegal activity

✅ Alternative measures

  • Human Development Index (HDI): Income + education + life expectancy
  • Gross National Happiness (Bhutan)
  • Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI): Adjusts GDP for inequality, environment
  • Gini Coefficient: Measures inequality (0 = equal, 1 = unequal)
