FUNDAMENTAL DISTINCTION

Microeconomics
vs Macroeconomics

Microeconomics vs Macroeconomics

🔬 Microeconomics

Studies how individual households and firms make decisions and interact in markets.

  • How does a consumer choose between goods?
  • How does a firm set its price?
  • How does the market for coffee work?

Individual markets

🌍 Macroeconomics

Studies the economy as a whole — aggregate variables and national-level phenomena.

  • Why is GDP growing or shrinking?
  • Why is there inflation?
  • How does monetary policy work?

Whole economy

TOOLS OF THE TRADE

Economic Models

How economists simplify reality to understand it

What Are Economic Models?

📘 Definition
An economic model is a simplified representation of reality used to understand, explain, and predict economic phenomena.

Models use assumptions to strip away unnecessary complexity and focus on key relationships.

Why simplify?

The real economy has millions of interacting agents. We can't model everything — so we focus on what matters most for the question at hand.

Analogy

A map of Ho Chi Minh City doesn't show every tree — it simplifies to show roads and landmarks. Economic models do the same.

Endogenous vs Exogenous Variables

🔵 Endogenous Variables

Variables the model tries to explain.

These are the outputs — determined within the model.

Examples: GDP, price level, quantity, employment, inflation

🟢 Exogenous Variables

Variables the model takes as given.

These are the inputs — determined outside the model.

Examples: Government policy, technology, weather, population

💡 Memory Trick
Endo = inside (the model explains it). Exo = outside (the model takes it as given). Think: endoskeleton (inside), exoskeleton (outside).

The Most Famous Model: Supply & Demand

This model describes the relationship between buyers and sellers in a market.

  • Demand curve: slopes downward — as price ↑, quantity demanded ↓
  • Supply curve: slopes upward — as price ↑, quantity supplied ↑
  • Equilibrium: where the curves intersect — the market clears
Endogenous: Price (P*), Quantity (Q*)
Exogenous: Income, technology, input prices, tastes

Market Clearing

📘 Definition
Market clearing is the process by which prices adjust so that the quantity supplied equals the quantity demanded (equilibrium).

How it works: Phở (noodle soup) example

  1. Initially, the market is at equilibrium: supply = demand at price P*
  2. Suppose income rises → demand for phở increases → demand shifts right
  3. At the old price P*, there is now excess demand (shortage)
  4. Price rises until a new equilibrium is reached at higher P and Q
⚠️ Important Assumption
Market clearing assumes flexible prices. In reality, prices and wages are often sticky (slow to adjust). This is a key insight for macroeconomics!

Shifts in Demand

A shift in demand means the entire curve moves (not a movement along the curve).

Demand shifts RIGHT when:

  • Income increases (normal goods)
  • Price of substitutes rises
  • Price of complements falls
  • Tastes/preferences increase
  • Population grows
  • Positive expectations about future

Result: Higher price, higher quantity

Demand shifts LEFT when:

  • Income decreases
  • Price of substitutes falls
  • Negative tastes/preferences
  • Population shrinks
  • Negative expectations

Result: Lower price, lower quantity

Shifts in Supply

Supply shifts RIGHT when:

  • Input prices fall (cheaper materials)
  • Technology improves
  • Number of sellers increases
  • Government subsidies
  • Favourable weather (agriculture)

Result: Lower price, higher quantity

Supply shifts LEFT when:

  • Input prices rise (expensive oil)
  • Natural disasters
  • New regulations/taxes
  • Number of sellers decreases

Result: Higher price, lower quantity

💡 VEO Exam Tip
Movement along a curve = caused by a change in the good's own price.
Shift of a curve = caused by a change in any other factor (income, input costs, etc.).

Flexible vs Sticky Prices

Flexible Prices

Adjust instantly to clear markets. Assumed in many microeconomic models and long-run macroeconomic models.

Examples: Stock prices, commodity prices (gold, oil), fresh food at markets

Sticky Prices

Adjust slowly due to contracts, menu costs, or coordination failures. Central to short-run macroeconomics.

Examples: Wages (set by annual contracts), restaurant menus, rental agreements

📘 Why This Matters
If all prices were perfectly flexible, the economy would always be at full employment. Because prices are sticky, the economy can deviate from full employment in the short run — which is why recessions happen and why government intervention (fiscal/monetary policy) can help.

Recessions and Depressions

📉 Recession

A period when real GDP falls for two or more consecutive quarters. Characterized by rising unemployment, falling consumer spending, and business pessimism.

Vietnam: GDP growth slowed to 2.91% in 2020 (lowest in decades due to COVID).

💥 Depression

A severe, prolonged recession. GDP falls sharply and recovery takes years.

Example: The Great Depression (1929–1939) — US GDP fell ~30%, unemployment reached 25%.

The Business Cycle

Economies naturally fluctuate between expansions and contractions:

Expansion: GDP rising, unemployment falling, confidence high
Contraction: GDP falling, unemployment rising, spending drops

CORE FRAMEWORK

The Circular Flow
of Income

The Simple Circular Flow

The economy can be viewed as a circular flow between households and firms:

🏠 Households
🏭 Firms
← Goods & Services ←
Firms sell to households
←
→ Spending ($$) →
Households pay firms
→
📘 Key Insight
Income ≡ Expenditure. Every dollar a household spends becomes income for a firm, which pays it out as wages/rent/profit to households. GDP measures this flow.

The Extended Circular Flow

The real economy includes more sectors:

Injections (add to the flow)

  • Investment (I): Firms spend on capital goods
  • Government spending (G): Public services, infrastructure
  • Exports (X): Foreign spending on domestic goods

Withdrawals (remove from the flow)

  • Saving (S): Income not spent on consumption
  • Taxation (T): Government takes income
  • Imports (M): Spending on foreign goods
📊 Equilibrium Condition
In equilibrium: Injections = Withdrawals, i.e., $I + G + X = S + T + M$

The GDP Identity

Y = C + I + G + NX

Components

  • C = Consumption (~65% of GDP)
  • I = Investment (~20%)
  • G = Government purchases (~15%)
  • NX = Net exports (X − M)

Three Equivalent Views

  • Total expenditure on goods & services
  • Total income earned in the economy
  • Total value added in production

All three = GDP

Economists as Policy Advisers

📘 Positive vs Normative Economics
  • Positive statements: Describe the world as it is. Can be tested. "Raising the minimum wage will reduce employment by 2%."
  • Normative statements: Describe how the world should be. Value judgments. "The government should raise the minimum wage."
🏆 The Truman Story

President Truman once said he wanted a "one-armed economist" because his advisers always said "On the one hand... On the other hand..."

This reflects that every policy involves trade-offs. Good economists acknowledge both costs and benefits — that's not a weakness, it's honest analysis.

Short Run vs Long Run

⏱️ Short Run
  • Prices and wages are sticky
  • Output can deviate from potential
  • AD matters — spending drives output
  • Unemployment can rise above natural rate
  • Government policy can affect real output

Keynesian world

📅 Long Run
  • Prices and wages are flexible
  • Output = potential output (full employment)
  • AS matters — supply determines output
  • Unemployment at natural rate
  • Money is neutral (affects prices, not output)

Classical world

🏆 Practice: Truman's One-Armed Economist

President Truman wanted a "one-armed economist" because economists always said "On one hand... on the other hand..." This observation:

  • (a) indicates economists recognise opportunity costs in policy decisions
  • (b) is rooted in the principle that people face trade-offs
  • (c) confirms economists are not suited to be advisers
  • (d) more than one answer is correct ✓
Both (a) and (b) are correct. Policy involves trade-offs (b) and opportunity costs (a). (c) is wrong — acknowledging trade-offs is good economics, not a flaw.

⚠️ Common Mistakes in VEO Exams

❌ Don't Do This
  • Confuse movement along a curve with a shift of the curve
  • Mix up micro and macro — "Samsung's profit" is NOT macro
  • Forget that GDP = Income = Expenditure (three equivalent views)
  • Assume all prices adjust instantly (they don't in the short run)
  • Confuse positive and normative statements
✅ Do This Instead
  • Always specify: "this causes the [demand/supply] curve to shift [left/right]"
  • Ask: "Is this about one market/firm, or the whole economy?"
  • Remember: Income ≡ Expenditure ≡ Value Added
  • Distinguish short run (sticky) from long run (flexible)
  • Look for "should" or "ought to" → normative
