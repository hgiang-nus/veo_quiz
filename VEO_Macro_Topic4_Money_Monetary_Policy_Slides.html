PART 1

What Is Money?

Not all wealth is money. Not all assets are money.

Definition of Money

Money = any commodity or token that is generally acceptable as a means of payment for goods and services.

⚠️ Common Misconception

Money ≠ wealth. Money ≠ income. Money ≠ all financial assets.

  • Wealth: Total assets (house, stocks, cash). A house is wealth but NOT money.
  • Income: Flow of earnings over time. Income is earned; money is held.
  • Money: Specifically the assets routinely accepted in transactions.

The Three Functions of Money

🔄

1. Medium of Exchange

Accepted in exchange for goods & services. Eliminates the need for barter (double coincidence of wants).

"I give you money; you give me phở."

📏

2. Unit of Account

Common measure to state prices and compare values of different goods.

"This laptop costs 15 million VND."

🏦

3. Store of Value

Can be held and exchanged later for goods and services. Transfers purchasing power over time.

"I save VND today, spend it next year."

Understanding Each Function

FunctionWithout moneyWith moneyExample
Medium of exchangeBarter: need "double coincidence of wants" — very inefficientBuy anything from anyone instantlyUsing VND to buy groceries
Unit of accountNeed exchange rates between every pair of goods (N goods → N(N-1)/2 prices!)Just N prices, all in one unitAll prices quoted in VND
Store of valueMust hold perishable goods or barter immediatelySave purchasing power for the futureSavings account in VND
⚠️ Inflation erodes the "store of value" function
If inflation is 10%, money held for a year loses 10% of its purchasing power. This is why people in high-inflation countries prefer to hold foreign currency or real assets.

Fractional-Reserve Banking

📘 Key Concept
Banks keep only a fraction of deposits as reserves and lend out the rest. This is how money gets created.

Reserve Ratio (rr)

$$rr = \frac{\text{Reserves}}{\text{Deposits}}$$

The required reserve ratio is set by the central bank. Banks must hold at least this fraction.

Example: If rr = 10%, a bank with $1,000 in deposits must keep $100 as reserves and can lend $900.

Why this creates money

When a bank lends $900, that $900 gets deposited in another bank. That bank keeps 10% ($90) and lends $810. The $810 gets deposited again...

Each loan creates a new deposit → the money supply expands beyond the original deposit!

The Money Multiplier

Money Multiplier $= \dfrac{1}{rr}$

Total Money Created = Initial Deposit × $\dfrac{1}{rr}$

Multiplier $= 1/0.20 = 5$

Total money = $1,000 × 5 = $5,000

🏆 VEO 2021: Money Multiplier

If all banks have a reserve ratio of 25%, then new reserves of 3 million VND can generate:

  • (a) 6 million VND
  • (b) 750,000 VND
  • (c) 12 million VND ✓
  • (d) 7.5 million VND

Solution:

Multiplier $= 1 / 0.25 = 4$

Total new money $= 3{,}000{,}000 \times 4 = $ 12,000,000 VND

🏆 VEO 2022: Loan Repayment

When a bank loan is repaid, the supply of money:

  • (a) may either increase or decrease
  • (b) is constant but composition changed
  • (c) is increased
  • (d) is decreased ✓
💡 Explanation
Bank lending creates money (new deposits). When loans are repaid, the process reverses: deposits are destroyed → money supply decreases. Think of it as "un-creating" money.

PART 3

Monetary Policy

How the central bank controls money supply and interest rates

What is Monetary Policy?

📘 Definition
Monetary policy = the control of the money supply and interest rates by the central bank to achieve macroeconomic goals (price stability, full employment, growth).
📈

Expansionary ("Easy money")

↑ Money Supply / ↓ Interest Rates

Goal: stimulate economy, fight recession

AD shifts right

📉

Contractionary ("Tight money")

↓ Money Supply / ↑ Interest Rates

Goal: cool economy, fight inflation

AD shifts left

The Central Bank's 3 Policy Tools

📊

1. Reserve Requirements

Min fraction of deposits banks must hold as reserves.

↓ rr → multiplier ↑ → MS ↑
↑ rr → multiplier ↓ → MS ↓

💰

2. Discount Rate

Interest rate CB charges banks for short-term loans.

↓ rate → banks borrow more → MS ↑
↑ rate → banks borrow less → MS ↓

📜

3. Open Market Operations

CB buys/sells government bonds.

Buy bonds → inject money → MS ↑
Sell bonds → absorb money → MS ↓

Most commonly used: Open Market Operations (OMO) — most flexible, can be done daily in any amount.

The Money Market

📘 Money Demand ($M^d$)

People hold money for transactions, precaution, and speculation. Money demand depends on:

  • Income (Y): ↑Y → ↑M^d (more transactions)
  • Interest rate (r): ↑r → ↓M^d (higher opportunity cost of holding cash)
  • Price level (P): ↑P → ↑M^d (need more money for same purchases)
📘 Money Supply ($M^s$)
Set by the central bank. Vertical line (does not depend on interest rate — CB chooses it).

How Money Supply Changes Affect Interest Rates

MS Increases (Expansionary)

Ms curve shifts right → surplus of money at old rate → interest rate falls to new equilibrium.

Lower r → more borrowing → more spending → AD shifts right

MS Decreases (Contractionary)

Ms curve shifts left → shortage of money at old rate → interest rate rises to new equilibrium.

Higher r → less borrowing → less spending → AD shifts left

📝 VEO Exam Tip
The chain is: MS → Interest rate → Investment/Consumption → AD → Output/Prices. Always trace through the full transmission mechanism in your answer.

of Money

The Quantity Theory of Money

$M \times V = P \times Y$

VariableMeaningDetermined by
MMoney supplyCentral bank
VVelocity of money (how many times each unit of money changes hands per year)Institutional factors (assumed constant in the short run)
PPrice levelEndogenous
YReal GDP (real output)Supply-side factors (in the long run)

QTM: Key Implications

If V is constant and Y is at full employment:

↑ M → ↑ P (proportionally)

"Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon." — Milton Friedman

📊 In growth rates:
$$\%\Delta M + \%\Delta V = \%\Delta P + \%\Delta Y$$

If V is constant ($\%\Delta V = 0$):

$$\text{Inflation} = \%\Delta M - \%\Delta Y$$

Example: Money supply grows 10%, real GDP grows 3% → Inflation ≈ 7%.

This is why printing money to finance government spending causes inflation (see VEO 2021 Q1c2!).

🇻🇳 SBV Monetary Policy in Practice

COVID-19 Response (2020–2021)
  • Three rate cuts (March, May, October 2020): discount rate to 2.5%/year, refinancing rate to 4%/year
  • Short-term lending rate cap lowered to 5%/year
  • Goal: reduce borrowing costs → stimulate investment → support recovery
  • Result: credit growth maintained; economy recovered faster than many peers
2022–2023: Tightening Cycle
  • Global inflation + Fed rate hikes → SBV raised rates to defend VND and control inflation
  • Credit growth targets tightened
  • Then eased again in 2023 to support recovery after real estate market stress
Vietnam's exchange rate regime: Managed float — SBV allows market forces but intervenes to prevent excessive volatility. VND is managed within a band against USD.

Limitations of Monetary Policy

LimitationDescription
Liquidity trapWhen interest rates are already near zero, CB can't lower them further. Extra money just gets hoarded. (Japan 1990s–2010s)
Pushing on a stringCB can make borrowing cheap, but can't force businesses to borrow or consumers to spend if confidence is low
Time lagsMonetary policy affects economy with 6–18 month delay. Hard to time correctly.
Asset bubblesLow rates can inflate stock/property prices → instability
Exchange rate effectsLowering rates can cause capital outflows → currency depreciation → imported inflation
InequalityLow rates benefit asset owners (stocks/property rise) more than workers

The Liquidity Trap

📘 Definition
A situation where interest rates are at or near zero, and monetary policy becomes ineffective. Increasing the money supply doesn't lower rates further — people just hold the extra cash.
⚠️ When this happens:
  • Conventional monetary policy is "trapped" — can't cut rates below zero (the "zero lower bound")
  • Central banks resort to unconventional tools: Quantitative Easing (QE) = buying long-term bonds to push down long-term rates
  • Fiscal policy becomes essential in a liquidity trap (Keynesian argument)
Examples: Japan (1990s–2020s), US and Europe after 2008 financial crisis, global response to COVID-19.

🏆 Practice: Money Multiplier Advanced

The SBV requires a reserve ratio of 10%. A new deposit of 500 million VND enters the banking system.

(a) What is the money multiplier?
(b) What is the maximum total money created?
(c) If the SBV raises the reserve ratio to 20%, what happens?

Solutions:

(a) $k = 1/0.10 = $ 10

(b) $500M \times 10 = $ 5,000 million VND = 5 billion VND

(c) New multiplier $= 1/0.20 = 5$. Max money $= 500M \times 5 = 2,500M$ VND. Money creation halved — this is contractionary.

🏆 Practice: Quantity Theory

Money supply grows at 12% per year. Real GDP grows at 5%. Velocity is constant. What is the inflation rate?

Solution:

$\%\Delta M + \%\Delta V = \%\Delta P + \%\Delta Y$

$12\% + 0\% = \pi + 5\%$

$\pi = 12\% - 5\% = $ 7%

Follow-up: The government prints money equal to 8% of GDP to finance a vaccine fund. What happens to inflation if real GDP growth stays at 5%?

Money growth = 8% (new) + whatever existing growth. If total M growth = 13%: π = 13% − 5% = 8%. Inflation jumps — this is why "printing money" is inflationary.

⚠️ Common Exam Mistakes

❌ Don't
  • Say "the CB prints money" as fiscal policy (that's monetary)
  • Forget that loan repayment destroys money
  • Confuse the money multiplier ($1/rr$) with the spending multiplier ($1/(1-MPC)$)
  • Say "CB controls interest rates directly" — CB controls money supply; interest rates adjust in the money market
  • Forget to trace the full transmission mechanism
✅ Do
  • Always specify which CB tool is used
  • Trace: Tool → MS → r → I/C → AD → Y/P
  • Distinguish short-run (r changes → real effects) vs long-run (money neutrality → only P changes)
  • Remember: higher rr → smaller multiplier → less money created
