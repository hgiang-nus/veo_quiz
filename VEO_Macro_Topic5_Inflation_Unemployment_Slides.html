Unemployment

Who counts, why it matters, what causes it

What is Unemployment?

📘 Definition
Unemployment = people of working age who are without work, available for work, and actively seeking work.
📘 Key Formulas
$$\text{Labour Force} = \text{Employed} + \text{Unemployed}$$$$\text{Adult Population} = \text{Labour Force} + \text{Not in Labour Force}$$

NOT in labour force: students, retirees, stay-at-home parents, discouraged workers.

Computing Unemployment Rate

$u = \dfrac{U}{L} \times 100\%$

📊 Example

Employed = 50M. Unemployed = 2.5M. L = 52.5M.

$u = 2.5/52.5 \times 100 = $ 4.76%

LFPR $= L / \text{Working-age Pop} \times 100\%$ — measures what fraction of potential workers participate.

🏆 VEO 2022: Unemployment Rate

1M part-time + 9M full-time workers. 500K part-time switch to full-time. Unemployment rate:

  • (a) Labour force increases
  • (b) Rate falls
  • (c) Rate unchanged ✓
  • (d) Rate rises
Both part-time and full-time = employed. Switching between them changes nothing about employment status, labour force size, or unemployment rate.

📘 Okun's Law
For every 1% unemployment above u*, real GDP is ~2% below potential: $(Y-Y^*)/Y^* \approx -2(u-u^*)$

Natural Rate of Unemployment ($u^*$)

📘 Definition
$u^* = $ frictional + structural. The "normal" unemployment even at full employment. Cyclical unemployment = 0 when u = u*.

At u = u*:

  • Full employment
  • Output at potential Y*
  • No inflation pressure from labour markets

u* varies by country

  • US: ~4–5%
  • Europe: ~6–8%
  • Vietnam: ~2–3% (large informal sector)

Inflation

Rising prices, causes, and costs

Prices increased 7–10× in 24 years. That's inflation.

What is Inflation?

📘 Inflation
A sustained increase in the general price level over time. Not just one good — the overall level.
📘 Deflation
A sustained decrease in the general price level. Rare but dangerous — deflationary spiral possible.
  • Disinflation: Inflation rate falling (8%→3%) but prices still rising
  • Hyperinflation: >50% per month (Zimbabwe 2008, Venezuela 2018)
  • Stagflation: High inflation + high unemployment + stagnant growth

Computing Inflation

$\pi_t = \dfrac{CPI_t - CPI_{t-1}}{CPI_{t-1}} \times 100\%$

📊 Example

CPI 2022 = 108.5. CPI 2023 = 112.0.

$\pi = (112.0-108.5)/108.5 \times 100 = $ 3.23%

🏆 VEO 2021: CPI Purpose

We calculate CPI in order to:

  • (a) Control wholesale prices
  • (b) Monitor changes in cost of living ✓
  • (c) Observe changes in real GDP
  • (d) Supervise stock market
CPI tracks cost of a fixed consumer basket over time = directly measures cost of living changes.

CAUSES OF INFLATION

Four Types

1. Demand-Pull Inflation

"Too much money chasing too few goods." Excess AD when economy near capacity.

Triggers

  • Consumer confidence surge → C↑
  • Government spending boom → G↑
  • Money supply expansion → r↓ → I↑
  • Tax cuts → Yd↑ → C↑

AD-AS: AD shifts RIGHT

P↑, Y↑ slightly (near capacity)

"Associated with a booming economy"

2. Cost-Push Inflation

Rising production costs push prices up from the supply side.

Triggers

  • Oil/energy price spikes
  • Wage increases above productivity
  • Currency depreciation → imports costlier
  • Supply chain disruptions (COVID)

AD-AS: SRAS shifts LEFT

P↑ AND Y↓ → Stagflation!

Vietnam 2022: Global oil/food prices + supply chains = cost-push pressure.

3. Structural & 4. Expectations Inflation

3. Structural

Demand shifts between sectors. Expanding sectors: wages↑. Contracting: wages sticky downward. Overall price level rises.

4. Expectations

If people expect inflation → demand higher wages → firms raise prices → self-fulfilling prophecy.

Why CB independence and credibility matter: anchor expectations.

Winners & Losers

Winners 📈

  • Borrowers: Repay with cheaper money
  • Government: Tax revenue↑; real debt shrinks
  • Homeowners with mortgages
  • Workers with strong unions

Losers 📉

  • Savers: Real value falls
  • Lenders/bondholders
  • Fixed-income earners (pensioners)
  • The poor: Higher % on basics

THE KEY TRADE-OFF

The Phillips Curve

Short-Run Phillips Curve

In the short run: trade-off between inflation and unemployment. Lower u → higher π.

Equation

$$\pi = \pi^e - \beta(u - u^*)$$
  • $\pi^e$ = expected inflation
  • $\beta$ = sensitivity
  • $u^*$ = natural rate

Long-Run Phillips Curve

In the long run: NO trade-off. LRPC is vertical at u*. Expectations adjust fully.

Why vertical?

  • Expectations adjust to actual inflation
  • Workers demand compensation → costs rise
  • Unemployment returns to u* at any inflation rate
  • Only unexpected inflation temporarily reduces u
When expected inflation rises, SRPC shifts up. Long run: always at u* — just at different π levels.

Sacrifice Ratio & Disinflation

$$\text{Sacrifice Ratio} = \frac{\% \text{ GDP lost}}{\% \text{ reduction in inflation}}$$Typically 3–5: reduce inflation by 1pp costs 3–5% of one year's GDP.

Reduce π from 8% to 3% (5pp). Sacrifice ratio = 4.

GDP sacrifice = 5 × 4 = 20% of one year's GDP. Huge cost!

Credibility matters: If CB is credible → expectations adjust quickly → sacrifice ratio lower.

🏆 Practice: Phillips Curve

$u^* = 5\%$, $\pi^e = 3\%$, $\beta = 0.5$. No supply shocks.

(a) u=3%: π=? (b) u=5%: π=? (c) u=8%: π=?

(a) $\pi = 3 - 0.5(3-5) = 3+1 = $ 4%

(b) $\pi = 3 - 0.5(5-5) = $ 3%

(c) $\pi = 3 - 0.5(8-5) = 3-1.5 = $ 1.5%

Policy Responses to Inflation

For Demand-Pull

  • Contractionary MP: ↑ rates → ↓ AD
  • Contractionary FP: ↓G or ↑T
  • Both shift AD left

For Cost-Push

  • Supply-side: ↓ input costs, ↑ productivity
  • Release strategic reserves
  • Reduce import tariffs
  • Dilemma: tighten→recession worse; loosen→inflation worse
Most important long-run tool: Anchor expectations through credible, independent CB.
