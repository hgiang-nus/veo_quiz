Topic 1
The Economic Problem

The Fundamental Problem

Unlimited wants vs. Limited resources

Economics studies how society manages scarce resources to fulfil wants — pursuing maximum satisfaction.

Scarcity & Choice

We cannot have it all → we must choose → every choice has an opportunity cost (the next best alternative forgone).

Vietnam: Building a new metro line in HCMC means fewer funds for rural hospitals — the hospital is the opportunity cost.

Topic 1
Three Questions & Economic Systems

What to produce?

Which goods with limited resources?

How to produce?

What technology/method?

For whom?

How to distribute output?

Market Economy

Private ownership, price signals. e.g. Singapore

Command Economy

State ownership, central plan. e.g. North Korea

Mixed Economy

Market + government. e.g. Vietnam

Micro vs. Macro

Microeconomics = individual units (consumers, firms, markets). Macroeconomics = aggregate economy (GDP, inflation, unemployment).

Vietnam's Doi Moi (1986): Shifted from command to mixed economy — market forces now answer "what" and "how", government guides "for whom" through social programs.

Topic 2 · Demand
Demand & Law of Demand

What is Demand?

The amount consumers are willing and able to purchase at each price.

Law of Demand

Ceteris paribus: Price ↑ → QD ↓. The demand curve slopes downward.

Key Distinction

Movement along = price changes QD.
Shift of curve = non-price factor changes demand.

Grab raises peak-hour prices in Hanoi → fewer rides booked (movement along). A new MRT line opens → demand for Grab falls at every price (shift left).

Topic 2 · Supply
Supply & The Law of Supply

Law of Supply

Ceteris paribus: Price ↑ → QS ↑. Positive relationship. Supply curve slopes upward.

Supply Shifters

Input prices · Technology · Taxes & subsidies · Prices of other goods · Expectations · Number of sellers · Weather

Vietnam coffee: A drought in Central Highlands reduces arabica yields → supply shifts left → world coffee prices rise. Vietnam is the 2nd-largest coffee exporter.

Topic 2 · Equilibrium
Market Equilibrium

Where D Meets S

Equilibrium: QD = QS → determines P* and Q*.

Practice Problem

QD = 120 − 2P   QS = 20 + 3P

120−2P = 20+3P → P*=20, Q*=80

Surplus & Shortage

If P > P* → surplus (QS > QD) → price falls
If P < P* → shortage (QD > QS) → price rises

Post-COVID Vietnam: Rising tourism demand (D→) + limited hotel supply → resort prices in Da Nang surged.

Price Ceiling & Price Floor

Ceiling: Vietnam's rent controls → housing shortages. Floor: Minimum wage (Vietnam's regional wages) → possible unemployment if set above equilibrium.

Topic 2 · Elasticity
Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

Definition

EP = %ΔQD / %ΔP

Midpoint: [(Q₂−Q₁)/avg Q] ÷ [(P₂−P₁)/avg P]

Elasticity & Total Revenue

Elastic (|E|>1): P↑→TR↓
Inelastic (|E|<1): P↑→TR↑
Unit (|E|=1): TR unchanged

Elastic: Luxury handbags (10% P↑ → >10% Q↓). Inelastic: Gasoline (20% P↑ → small Q↓, people still drive).

Topic 2 · Elasticity
Cross-Price & Income Elasticity

Cross-Price Elasticity (XED)

EX,Y = %ΔQX / %ΔPY
  • E > 0 → Substitutes
  • E < 0 → Complements
  • E = 0 → Unrelated

Income Elasticity (YED)

EI = %ΔQD / %ΔIncome
  • E > 1 → Normal, luxury
  • 0 < E < 1 → Normal, necessity
  • E < 0 → Inferior good

Price Elasticity of Supply

ES = %ΔQS / %ΔP

Elastic (>1): manufacturing. Inelastic (<1): agriculture, land.

XED: VinFast e-scooters & petrol scooters are substitutes (E>0). YED: As income grows, spending on travel (luxury, E>1) rises faster than on rice (necessity, 0

Theory of Consumer Choice

Topic 3
Budget Constraint & Indifference Curves

Budget Constraint

PX·X + PY·Y = I

Slope = −PX/PY. Income↑ → parallel shift out. Price change → pivot.

Indifference Curve Properties

① Higher = preferred ② Downward-sloping ③ Don't cross ④ Convex (diminishing MRS)

A student with S$500/month: the budget line shows trade-offs between food and transport. The optimal bundle is where the highest indifference curve just touches the budget line.

Topic 3
Utility & Diminishing Marginal Utility

Utility = Satisfaction

TU: Total satisfaction from Q units.
MU: Extra satisfaction from one more.

MU = ΔTU / ΔQ

Law of Diminishing MU

As Q↑, each extra unit gives less extra satisfaction. MU declines.

Utility Maximization

MUX/PX = MUY/PY

Equal marginal utility per dollar across all goods.

Bubble tea: 1st cup on a hot day = enormous pleasure. 4th cup = barely enjoy it. That's diminishing MU!

Topic 3
Income & Substitution Effects

Substitution Effect

Good becomes relatively cheaper → buy more of it, less of others. (Utility held constant)

Income Effect

Price fall → real purchasing power increases → can buy more of all goods. (Relative price held constant)

Normal vs Inferior

Normal: Both effects reinforce → P↓ always means Q↑.
Inferior: Effects work against each other. Substitution effect usually dominates (Giffen goods are the rare exception).

Vietnam Airlines fare discount: Substitution effect — fly more instead of bus. Income effect — money saved allows an extra trip overall.

Topic 04
Theory of the Firm
Production, short-run & long-run costs, economies of scale.
Topic 4
Production Function & Diminishing Returns

Key Concepts

Q = F(K, L)   π = TR − TC

TP: Total product   MPL = ΔQ/ΔL   APL = Q/L

Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns

As more labour is added to fixed capital, MPL eventually declines.

Phở restaurant: 1 kitchen + 2nd cook doubles output. By 6th cook, they're bumping into each other — MP falls.

Topic 4
Short-Run Cost Curves

Cost Structure

TC = TFC + TVC

AFC=TFC/Q   AVC=TVC/Q   ATC=TC/Q

MC = ΔTC / ΔQ

Key Rule

MC intersects ATC and AVC at their minimum points.
MC < ATC → ATC falling
MC > ATC → ATC rising

Cost Curve Shifters

Input prices · Technology · Taxes & subsidies

Grab driver: Fixed = loan payment. Variable = fuel per km. AFC falls as you drive more, but MC rises in heavy traffic.

Topic 4
Long-Run ATC & Economies of Scale

Long Run: All Inputs Variable

LRATC = envelope of all short-run ATC curves.

Economies

LRATC↓ as Q↑

Constant

LRATC flat

Diseconomies

LRATC↑ as Q↑

Viettel: Spreads network costs over 80M+ subscribers — huge economies of scale. But managing such a large organization can create bureaucratic diseconomies.

Topic 5
Perfect Competition: Profit Maximization

Key: P = MR = AR

Price taker → horizontal demand at market price. Profit max: P = MR = MC.

Long-Run Equilibrium

Entry/exit → P = ATC → zero economic profit. Accounting profit is still positive (covers opportunity costs).

Mekong Delta rice farmers: Thousands sell identical rice. No single farmer can influence the price. If profitable, new farmers enter → profit erodes to zero.

Topic 5
Monopoly: Pricing & Output

Key Features

Single seller · Unique product · Blocked entry · Price maker. Faces market demand curve.

Critical: P > MR

To sell more, must lower price on all units → MR curve lies below demand. Profit max: MR = MC, but P > MC.

EVN (Vietnam Electricity): Natural monopoly in distribution. Building a 2nd grid would be wastefully duplicative. Government regulates prices.

Topic 5
Price Discrimination & Monopolistic Competition

Price Discrimination

Charging different prices for the same product. Requires: ① Market power ② Segregable buyers ③ No resale.

Monopolistic Competition

Many sellers · Differentiated products · Easy entry/exit · Downward-sloping demand. SR: MR=MC, possible profit. LR: zero economic profit (P=ATC).

Vietnam Airlines: Same Hanoi–HCMC flight charges 3-4x more for last-minute business travelers vs. early-booking leisure travelers. Classic price discrimination.

Hanoi's Old Quarter cafés: Hundreds of cafés, each slightly different (egg coffee, coconut coffee, aesthetic decor). Easy entry → new shops weekly. If one charges too much, customers switch.

Topic 5
Oligopoly & Game Theory

Oligopoly Features

Few large firms · Mutual interdependence · Significant barriers · Possible collusion (cartels).

Game Theory Concepts

Nash Equilibrium: No player can improve by changing unilaterally.
Dominant Strategy: Best regardless of rival's choice.

Vietnam telecom: Viettel, VinaPhone, MobiFone — when Viettel cuts data prices, others must respond. Classic oligopolistic interdependence.

Topic 5
Game Theory: Business Application

Analysis

Both have dominant strategy: Discount. Nash: (Discount, Discount) = ($4B, $3B). But cooperation (Regular, Regular) = ($6B, $4B) is better for both — the classic dilemma.

Cartels

Firms may collude (act as monopolist). But cartels are unstable: incentive to cheat, costly to form, new entrants.

OPEC: Oil nations agree to cut production but each has incentive to produce more — cartels are notoriously unstable.

Topic 06
Market Failures & Government
Externalities, public goods, information asymmetries — when markets fail and how government can intervene.
Topic 6
Negative Externalities

Social Cost > Private Cost

MSC = MPC + MEC

Market overproduces: QE > QO. Creates deadweight loss.

Solutions

① Regulation ② Pigovian tax (= external cost) ③ Tradable permits (cap-and-trade)

Hanoi air pollution: Factory & motorbike emissions impose health costs on residents. Vietnam's Environmental Protection Tax on plastic bags (₫50,000/kg) is a real Pigovian tax.

Topic 6
Positive Externalities

Social Benefit > Private Benefit

MSB = MPB + MEB

Market underproduces: QE < QO. Creates deadweight loss.

Solutions

① Subsidies to buyers (↑D) ② Subsidies to producers (↑S) ③ Public provision (gov't provides directly)

Education in Vietnam: Student gains skills (private benefit) + society gets productive workforce (external benefit). Without subsidies, fewer would pursue higher education than is optimal.

Topic 6
Public Goods & the Free-Rider Problem

Vietnam's sea dyke system: Protects millions from flooding — non-rival and non-excludable. Only government can provide. Common resource: Overfishing in the South China Sea — rival but non-excludable → tragedy of the commons.

Topic 6
Asymmetric Information

Moral Hazard

After a contract: one party changes behavior costly to the other. Hidden action.

e.g. After getting insurance, taking fewer precautions

Adverse Selection

Before a transaction: informed party's hidden characteristics lead to bad outcomes. Hidden information.

e.g. Used car market — "Market for Lemons"

Private Solutions (Coase Theorem)

If property rights are well-defined and transaction costs low, private parties can negotiate efficient outcomes. Also: moral codes, charities, contracts. But often government intervention is still needed.

Moral hazard: Health insurance → less careful. Adverse selection: Vietnam's used car market — sellers know defects, cautious buyers offer low prices, good cars leave market.

Topic 6
Government Solutions Summary

Neg. Externalities

  • Pigovian tax
  • Regulation
  • Cap-and-trade

Pos. Externalities

  • Subsidies (buyer/seller)
  • Public provision

Public Goods

  • Gov't provision
  • Tax-funded

Cap-and-Trade (Tradable Permits)

Gov't sets pollution cap → issues permits → firms trade them. Firms that reduce cheaply sell permits; firms where reduction is expensive buy. Total cost minimized.

EU ETS: World's largest carbon market, ~10,000 plants. Vietnam is developing its own ETS (target 2028-29) as part of net-zero by 2050 commitment. Gov't subsidizes vaccinations (positive externality — herd immunity).

Exam
Common Mistakes to Avoid
✗

"Demand increased" vs. "Quantity demanded increased"

Shift (whole curve) ≠ movement along curve. Always specify.

✗

Elastic vs Inelastic on TR

Elastic: P↑→TR↓. Inelastic: P↑→TR↑. Draw a table if unsure.

✗

Monopoly always profits

Wrong! If demand is weak and costs high, a monopolist can make losses.

✗

Zero profit = failing firm

Zero economic profit means covering ALL costs including opportunity costs. Accounting profit is still positive.

✗

Ceiling→surplus / Floor→shortage

Opposite! Ceiling (max) → shortage. Floor (min) → surplus.

Exam
How to Ace Diagram Questions

5-Step Method for Market Analysis

  • Step 1: Draw original equilibrium (D, S, P*, Q*) — label everything
  • Step 2: Identify the shock — demand or supply factor?
  • Step 3: Shift the correct curve in the correct direction
  • Step 4: Find new equilibrium — label P**, Q**
  • Step 5: State conclusion clearly

Externality Diagram Checklist

① Label MPC (=S), MPB (=D), and MSC or MSB ② Show market QE and social QO ③ Shade deadweight loss triangle ④ State over- or under-production

Pro tip: Even if your graph isn't perfect, correctly labeled axes (P on y, Q on x) and clear shift arrows earn significant marks. Always annotate!
