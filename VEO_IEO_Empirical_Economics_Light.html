01

Nobel Prize

Nobel 2021: Does More School = More Money?

🏅 The Nobel Story

In 2021, David Card, Joshua Angrist, and Guido Imbens won the Nobel Prize for showing economists how to answer cause-and-effect questions using natural experiments — real-world situations that mimic a lab experiment.

Their biggest question? Does going to school longer actually make you earn more — or do smart people just stay in school longer?

Everyone assumes education pays off. But proving it is surprisingly hard — because the students who go to college are already different from those who don't.

— The identification problem
🎯 Why It's Tricky

Imagine comparing college graduates to non-graduates. Graduates earn more. But is that because:

  • 📚 College taught them useful skills? (human capital)
  • 🧠 They were already smarter/more motivated? (selection bias)
  • 📜 The degree just signals ability to employers? (signaling)
The Big Idea

You can't just compare people who went to college with people who didn't. You need to find situations where some people accidentally got more education than others — then see if they earned more.

01

Clever Trick #1

Quarter-of-Birth: When Your Birthday Decides Your Education

📅 Angrist & Krueger (1991)

Here's a fun fact: in the US, kids born in January start school almost a year older than kids born in December. Since the law says you can drop out at age 16, January-born kids can leave school earlier — with slightly less education.

The trick: Your birthday is basically random. So this creates a natural experiment!

~0.1 yrless schooling for Q1-born
7-8%return per extra year
~6%naive OLS estimate
💡 What We Learned

Surprise: The IV estimate (7-8%) was higher than the simple regression (6%). This suggests the students forced to stay in school by birthday luck actually benefited a lot.

But Wait...

Later researchers found problems: winter births are more common among disadvantaged families. So birthday may not be truly random after all. Lesson: even clever research designs can have hidden flaws.

VEO/IEO Tip

This is the perfect example to cite when a question asks about instrumental variables or natural experiments.

01

Clever Trick #2

Living Near a College = Going to College?

🏫 Card (1995)

Idea: People who grew up near a college are more likely to attend — simply because it's cheaper and easier. But where you grow up doesn't directly affect your future wages (in theory).

~12%IV return per year
~7%simple OLS return
0.32 yrextra schooling if near college
🔑 The Key Insight

The return is higher for students at the margin — those who wouldn't have attended college without the proximity advantage. These tend to be lower-income students.

If the students who barely make it to college benefit the most, what does that tell us about expanding access?
Policy Implication

Building colleges in underserved areas isn't just about fairness — it's about efficiency. The marginal student has the highest return on investment.

01

Early Childhood

The Best Investment You Can Make: Ages 0–5

👶 The Heckman Curve

James Heckman (Nobel 2000) showed that the rate of return on education spending is highest in early childhood and declines with age.

Think of it like compound interest — skills build on skills. A child who enters school ready to learn gains more from every subsequent year.

7-10%annual social ROI for preschool
+$11Kannual earnings at age 40 (Perry Preschool)
-44%arrest rate for treated group
🧩 Why Early > Late

The Perry Preschool experiment (1962) randomly assigned low-income children to preschool. Follow-up at age 40 showed massive benefits.

The twist: IQ effects faded by age 8. But the real gains came from non-cognitive skills — patience, self-control, working with others. These are easier to shape in young children.

Vietnam Context

Vietnam's preschool enrollment is ~93% for 5-year-olds but only ~30% for under-3s. Expanding access in rural and ethnic minority areas may be the single highest-return public investment available.

01

Teacher Quality

One Great Teacher Can Change Everything

👩‍🏫 Chetty, Friedman & Rockoff (2014)

Using tax records linked to school data, Chetty found that replacing a below-average teacher with an above-average one would increase a single classroom's lifetime earnings by $320,000.

Students with better kindergarten teachers were also more likely to attend college, less likely to become teen parents, and saved more for retirement.

🌏 What Works in Developing Countries?
  • 🪱 Deworming (Kenya): $3.50/student → attendance ↑25%. One of the cheapest interventions ever measured
  • 📱 Adaptive learning software (India): Mindspark app personalizes to each student's level → +0.37 SD in math in 4.5 months
  • 💰 Conditional cash transfers (Mexico): Pay families if kids attend school → girls' enrollment ↑9.3%
  • 📖 Textbooks alone (Kenya): No effect! Why? Books were in English; students spoke local languages. Inputs without pedagogy don't work.
J-PAL Cost-Effectiveness

Per $100 spent, the ranking: structured teaching methods > adaptive technology > deworming > teacher training. Reducing class size is surprisingly low on the list.

01

The Signaling Debate

Is College About Learning or Showing Off?

📚 Human Capital View

Becker (1964): Education builds skills that make you more productive. Higher wages reflect real ability gained in school.

Evidence: Returns vary by field (STEM > humanities), employer learning reduces the signaling premium over time, and test score gains predict wage gains.

📜 Signaling View

Spence (1973): Education just reveals pre-existing ability. The degree is a costly signal — smart people find it cheaper to get.

Evidence: "Sheepskin effects" — wages jump at diploma/degree completion, not gradually per year. If it were about learning, each year should count equally.

The Verdict

Probably both. Early education (literacy, numeracy) is mostly human capital. College may be 50-80% signaling for some fields. Even if partly signaling, education still helps individuals — but the social return is lower than the private return.

01

Inequality & Mobility

The Great Gatsby Curve: Why Inequality Sticks

📊 Chetty's Mobility Atlas

Key finding: Where you grow up in America matters enormously. Children in some neighborhoods have a 75% chance of moving up; in others, just 4%.

What predicts upward mobility? School quality, low segregation, two-parent families, social capital, less inequality.

A child born to parents in the bottom 20% in Dubuque, Iowa has a better chance of reaching the top 20% than a child born in Charlotte, North Carolina. Geography is destiny — unless policy intervenes.

🌏 Cross-Country Evidence
  • 🇫🇮 Finland: Abolished tracking in the 1970s → inequality fell 23% with no loss in average achievement
  • 🇻🇳 Vietnam's paradox: PISA scores in the top 20 globally despite GDP/capita in the bottom 1/3 of test-takers. How? High parental aspirations, focused curriculum, teacher commitment
  • ⚠️ But: Urban-rural and ethnic gaps remain large — Kinh students outperform ethnic minorities by ~1 standard deviation
The Big Picture

More equal education → more equal outcomes → more social mobility. This is where economics of education meets inequality.

01

EdTech & AI

Will AI Replace Teachers? Not So Fast

🤖 Early Evidence on AI in Education
  • Adaptive learning (Mindspark): +0.37 SD because it meets students at their level — not the curriculum's level
  • AI tutoring: Students using Socratic-style AI tutors scored +0.2 SD better — but only when AI guided rather than answered
  • MOOCs: Only 5-10% complete online courses. Learners are mostly already educated. Not the great equalizer hoped for.
⚡ The Real Question
Should schools teach students how to use AI, or teach them skills that AI cannot do?

Early evidence suggests AI compresses the skill distribution — weak students gain the most. But students may learn to prompt rather than think.

ASEAN Opportunity

700 million people, many in areas with teacher shortages. AI-powered adaptive learning via smartphones could leapfrog traditional infrastructure. Vietnam's 79% internet penetration makes it a prime candidate.

01

COVID Learning Loss

The Pandemic's Hidden Scar: What Kids Lost

🦠 The Numbers
-0.14 SDaverage global learning loss
-0.29 SDloss for low-SES students
~2 yrsto recover (optimistic estimate)

Betthäuser et al. (2023): Meta-analysis across 42 countries. COVID didn't just close schools — it widened every existing gap. Low-income students, girls in developing countries, and students with disabilities were hit hardest.

💡 What Helped vs. What Didn't
  • ✅ Structured tutoring (UK National Tutoring Programme): +0.1–0.2 SD recovery
  • ✅ Extended school time (summer programs): moderate effects
  • ❌ Just distributing laptops: minimal effect without teacher training and content
  • ❌ Online-only instruction: 50-70% less effective than in-person for young children
Key Lesson

Technology enables learning but doesn't cause it. The teacher-student relationship, structured curriculum, and face-to-face interaction remain irreplaceable — even in the AI age.

OECD. 80+ countries, 15-year-olds. Math, reading, science. Vietnam participates since 2012.

Oxford. 12,000 children in Ethiopia, India, Peru, Vietnam. Rich longitudinal data.

LSMS, DHS, enterprise surveys. Cross-country comparable.

  • 📏 Years ≠ quality: A year of school in Finland ≠ a year in Mali. Test scores predict growth better than years.
  • 📉 53% of children in low/middle-income countries can't read a simple text by age 10 ("learning poverty")

Use administrative data (school records, tax records) when possible. Chetty's breakthroughs came from linking IRS data to school records — impossible with surveys alone.

02

Nobel Prize

Nobel 2018: What's the Right Price for Destroying the Planet?

🏅 William Nordhaus

Nordhaus won for building the first model that links the economy to the climate. His DICE model asks: if burning fossil fuels causes future damage, what tax should we charge today?

Climate change is the biggest market failure in human history. The atmosphere is a free dumping ground — nobody pays for the damage. Nordhaus figured out how to calculate the bill.

— The externality problem
💰 The Carbon Tax Debate
$50Nordhaus optimal tax (2020$)
$185US EPA estimate (2023)
$80-400range in the literature

Why the huge range? It all comes down to one number: the discount rate — how much we value the future.

The Ethical Core

Nordhaus (1.5% discount rate): follow what markets tell us people actually value. Stern (0.1%): future generations matter equally — we have a moral obligation to act aggressively. A seemingly technical parameter drives trillion-dollar policy differences.

02

Carbon Pricing 101

Carbon Tax vs. Cap-and-Trade: The Two Big Tools

💰 Option A: Carbon Tax

Put a price on each ton of CO₂. Companies decide how much to reduce emissions based on the cost.

✅ Pros

Price certainty, simple, raises revenue

❌ Cons

Emissions uncertain — what if firms just pay and pollute?

Examples: Sweden ($130/tCO₂ — world's highest), British Columbia ($65 CAD)

🔄 Option B: Cap-and-Trade (ETS)

Set a cap on total emissions. Issue permits. Companies trade them.

✅ Pros

Emissions certainty, creates a market, permits can be auctioned

❌ Cons

Price volatile — can crash to zero (EU Phase I) or spike unpredictably

Examples: EU ETS (€65-90/tCO₂), China ETS (~$10/tCO₂)

Weitzman's Rule (1974)

Use a tax when damage is predictable. Use a cap when there are dangerous thresholds (tipping points). For climate: tipping points argue for quantity controls.

02

EU ETS

The EU Emissions Trading System: A Cautionary Tale

📉 Phase I: How Free Permits Crashed the Market

2005-07: EU gave away permits for free based on firms' self-reported emissions. Firms inflated their baselines → too many permits → supply > demand → price crashed to €0 in 2007.

Lesson: Free allocation based on self-reporting = recipe for gaming.

Companies literally made money from a system designed to make pollution costly. It took the EU 15 years to fix this.

📈 Phase III-IV: How It Got Fixed
  • Centralized allocation replaced self-reporting
  • Auctioning introduced (~57% of permits sold)
  • Market Stability Reserve absorbs surplus permits
  • Price rose from €5 → €65-100 by 2023
-10%emissions reduction vs. counterfactual
+10%clean patenting by regulated firms
€65-90current carbon price (2024)
Vietnam Lesson

Vietnam's ETS pilot (2025-28) uses 100% free allocation — exactly like EU Phase I. Must build robust monitoring before transitioning to auctioning.

02

Vietnam ETS

Vietnam's Emissions Trading System: Decree 119/2025

🇻🇳 The Architecture

Pilot Phase (2025-2028):

  • Sectors: power, steel, cement
  • All permits free (grandfathered)
  • Government auction revenue: effectively zero
  • Focus: building MRV capacity (Monitoring, Reporting, Verification)

Full Operation (post-2029): Gradual auctioning, expanded coverage, potential linking with regional markets.

🔑 Key Design Questions
If all permits are free, does the ETS actually reduce emissions?

Yes, but less effectively. Free allocation still creates a market — firms that reduce cheaply can sell permits to those facing higher costs. But without auctioning, the government misses revenue that could fund green investment.

~300 MtCO₂ covered annually
$0auction revenue (pilot)
$1-3Bpotential if fully auctioned
CBAM Connection

EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism charges importers. If Vietnam's ETS is credible, Vietnamese steel exporters avoid CBAM costs. This is the strongest incentive for Vietnam to price carbon seriously.

02

Green Innovation

Can We Innovate Our Way Out of Climate Change?

💡 Directed Technical Change

Acemoglu et al. (2012): Innovation can be redirected from fossil fuels to clean energy — but it requires both a carbon tax AND R&D subsidies. Neither alone is sufficient.

Why? Path dependence — past fossil fuel innovation makes future fossil innovation cheaper. Breaking the path requires a push from both sides.

+10%clean patents from EU ETS
2×faster growth of clean vs. dirty innovation since 2005
$370BUS IRA clean energy subsidies (2022)
🌿 Is Green Growth Possible?
  • ✅ Optimists: Solar is now cheaper than coal in most markets. EV costs fell 90% in a decade. Decoupling is happening.
  • ⚠️ Skeptics: Rebound effects — efficiency gains get eaten by increased consumption. Absolute decoupling not yet achieved globally.
  • 🇻🇳 Vietnam opportunity: World's #3 solar PV exporter. Green hydrogen potential. VinFast EVs. R&D tax credits + FDI in clean tech could position Vietnam as a green manufacturing hub.
Key Insight

Carbon pricing incentivizes incremental improvements. R&D subsidies enable radical breakthroughs. Climate policy needs both carrots and sticks.

02

Air Pollution

The Silent Killer: Air Pollution & Health

🏭 Stunning Natural Experiments
  • 🇨🇳 Ebenstein et al. (2017): China's Huai River heating policy → free coal heating north of the river → PM10 is 55% higher → life expectancy is 3.1 years shorter in the north
  • 🇺🇸 Currie & Walker (2011): E-ZPass electronic tolling reduced vehicle idling near toll plazas → premature births fell 10.8% within 2km
  • 🌡️ Burke et al. (2015): Unmitigated warming could reduce global GDP by 23% by 2100
🇻🇳 Vietnam: A Personal Issue

Hanoi regularly ranks in the world's top 5 most polluted capitals. PM2.5 averages 35-45 μg/m³ — 7-9× the WHO guideline.

5-7%of GDP: economic cost of air pollution in Vietnam
7-9×above WHO safe level (Hanoi PM2.5)
3.1 yrslife expectancy lost (China Huai River study)
Why This Matters for Economics

Environmental regulation has real costs (Greenstone: $75B in US manufacturing). But the health benefits are enormous and often underestimated. Benefit-cost analysis consistently shows pollution regulation is worth it.

02

Behavioral & Nudges

Green Nudges: Small Pushes, Big Effects

🧠 Nudges That Work
  • 💡 Opower home energy reports: "Your neighbors use less electricity than you" → 2% reduction. Scaled to 6M+ households. Equivalent to an 11-20% energy price increase!
  • 🏨 Hotel towel reuse: "75% of guests reuse towels" beats "save the planet" (+26% vs +16%). Social norms > moral appeals.
  • 🍔 Smart green nudging (von Zahn et al. 2025): AI-powered food delivery nudges → -10% carbon per order with minimal satisfaction loss
⚡ The Rebound Problem

When you buy a fuel-efficient car, you might drive more. When your house is well-insulated, you might crank the heating.

Gillingham et al. (2016): Rebound effect is ~10-30% for vehicles. Not catastrophic, but real.

Bottom Line

Nudges are cost-effective complements to carbon pricing. They work for small daily decisions. But for deep decarbonization (closing coal plants, redesigning cities), you need prices and regulation.

Climate Finance

Carbon Markets, Green Bonds & Stranded Assets

💹 Climate Finance
  • 💚 Green bonds: $500B+ issued in 2023. But "greenwashing" concerns — are companies really going green?
  • 📈 Carbon premium: Bolton & Kacperczyk (2021) — high-emission stocks earn 1-2% annual premium. Markets are partially pricing climate risk.
  • 🏦 Central bank stress tests: ECB, Bank of England, MAS now test banks for climate risk
🔥 Stranded Assets

If the 2°C target is met, 80% of fossil fuel reserves cannot be burned. This implies massive write-downs for oil, gas, and coal companies.

$2.5Tfinancial assets at risk at 2.5°C
80%of reserves that become stranded
$30-40B/yrVietnam's climate finance gap
Vietnam

Vietnam needs $30-40B/year for climate adaptation. Current climate finance: ~$5B. First sovereign green bond planned for 2025-26. The gap is enormous.

📏 RDD

EPA regulation thresholds — counties just above vs. below pollution limits (Greenstone 2002)

But follow-up work showed infant mortality fell significantly in those same counties. The health benefits may well exceed the economic costs.

03

Nobel Prize

Nobel 2023: Why Do Women Still Earn Less?

🏅 Claudia Goldin

The first woman to win the Economics Nobel solo. Goldin spent 40 years analyzing 200 years of US data on women in the workforce.

Her surprising conclusion: the remaining gender pay gap is not mainly about discrimination. It's about how the modern economy rewards very long, inflexible hours.

84¢women earn per $1 men earn (US)
~73%female LFP (Vietnam)
132 yrsto close global gender gap (WEF)
📉 The U-Shaped Curve

Female labor force participation followed a U-shape over 200 years:

  • 1800s (farm era): Women worked alongside men — high participation
  • 1850-1920 (factories): Physical work, stigma for married women — participation dropped
  • 1950s-today (services): Education rose, the Pill arrived, norms changed — participation climbed back
Vietnam

Vietnam's ~73% female LFP is among the world's highest. But women are concentrated in agriculture and low-wage manufacturing. The quality gap matters more than the quantity gap.

03

The Pill

The Pill Changed Everything — Here's How

💊 Goldin & Katz (2002)

The contraceptive pill (approved 1960) gave women reliable control over when to have children for the first time. This enabled career investment — women could delay childbirth to finish education and establish careers.

+2.1 yrsdelay in first birth
+30%women entering law, medicine, MBA
+8%higher wages at age 40 (Bailey 2006)
🔑 Why Timing Is Everything

Before the Pill, women expected short careers → chose "flexible" jobs (teaching, nursing). After the Pill, career horizons lengthened → women entered "greedy" careers (law, finance, medicine).

It's not just about having fewer children. It's about the option value of controlling when. Today's parallel: IVF and egg freezing extend the fertility window further.

Bigger Lesson

Technology can reshape labor markets by changing expectations about career horizons. This is a theme that connects to AI's impact on work (Topic 7).

03

Greedy Jobs

The 'Greedy Jobs' Problem: Why Finance Has a Bigger Gap Than Pharmacy

💼 Goldin (2014)

"Greedy" occupations disproportionately reward long, unpredictable hours. Working 80 hours in finance pays more than 2× working 40. In pharmacy? Almost exactly 2×.

~0%gender gap in pharmacy
~40%gender gap in finance (age 45)
If the gender gap is about job design, not discrimination, what's the solution?
🔑 Redesign Work, Not Women

Goldin's answer: make workers more substitutable. When any pharmacist can replace any other, there's no premium for being the one who's always there.

  • COVID natural experiment: Firms that adopted remote work saw gender gap narrow in promotions
  • But: "Flexibility stigma" — workers who use flex arrangements get penalized in evaluations
The Radical Implication

Equal pay laws won't close the last chapter of the gender gap. What's needed: redesigning jobs so that flexibility isn't punished. This is about organizational design, not legislation.

03

Child Penalty

The Child Penalty: Motherhood Costs Women 20% of Their Earnings

👶 Kleven, Landais & Søgaard (2019)

Track men and women's earnings before and after their first child. In Denmark:

-20%long-run earnings drop for mothers
~0%drop for fathers
80%of the gender gap explained by children

The penalty operates through: some mothers exit work, some reduce hours, some switch to lower-paying flexible jobs.

🌍 Replicated in 134 Countries
  • 🇩🇰 Nordic countries: ~20-25% penalty (despite great family policies!)
  • 🇩🇪 Germany/Austria: ~50-60% (half-day school forces mothers home)
  • 🇯🇵 Japan/Korea: ~60-70% (very strong traditional norms)
  • 🇺🇸 US/UK: ~30-40%
Vietnam Research Gap

No comparable child penalty study exists for Vietnam. VHLSS panel data could support one. Hypothesis: the extensive margin penalty (leaving work) may be low, but the wage rate penalty (switching to lower-paid jobs) could be high.

03

Policy Experiments

What Policies Help? Parental Leave, Quotas, Childcare

👨‍👧 Parental Leave Design
  • 🇸🇪 Sweden "daddy months": Reserved leave for fathers → male uptake jumped from 0.5% to 25%
  • 🇩🇪 Germany reform (2007): Shorter but more generous leave → mothers returned faster → earnings recovered
  • ⚠️ Very long leave (2-3 years) hurts: Skill depreciation, career derailment
Optimal Design

6-12 months of moderate leave + ‘use-it-or-lose-it’ father quotas. Short enough to avoid skill depreciation, generous enough to support families.

🏛️ Quotas & Representation
  • 🇮🇳 India village councils: Random gender quotas → female leaders invest more in water and roads (Chattopadhyay & Duflo 2004)
  • 🇳🇴 Norway board quotas: 40% female boards mandated. But no trickle-down to women below board level (Bertrand et al. 2019)
  • 🎯 Blind auditions: Goldin & Rouse (2000) — orchestras added screens → women's hiring probability ↑50%
Takeaway

Quotas increase representation but don't automatically change workplace culture. Blind evaluation reduces bias. Parental leave design matters as much as generosity.

03

Norms & Culture

Gender Norms Travel Across Generations and Borders

🌐 Culture Matters
  • Fernández & Fogli (2009): Second-generation immigrant women in the US have LFP rates correlated with their parents' country of origin. Culture persists.
  • Jensen & Oster (2009): Cable TV in rural India → exposure to urban lifestyles → female autonomy increased, girls stayed in school longer
  • Beaman et al. (2012): Exposure to female leaders changed parental aspirations for daughters — gender gap in aspirations fell 25%
🇻🇳 Vietnam Gender Norms

Vietnam has relatively egalitarian norms (women's role in independence wars, socialist ideology). But:

  • 👩‍🍳 Double burden: women work AND do 80% of housework
  • 👦 Son preference persists (sex ratio at birth: 112:100, 2022)
  • 💼 Women underrepresented in management and National Assembly (~30%)
Key Insight

Norms are powerful but not permanent. Media, role models, institutional design, and policy can shift them — but it takes sustained effort over decades.

Nobel Prize

Nobel 2019: Can We Actually Measure What Works Against Poverty?

🏅 Banerjee, Duflo & Kremer

They won for "their experimental approach to alleviating global poverty." Instead of asking "what causes economic growth?", they asked "does this specific program help these specific people?"

The old approach: run a regression of GDP growth on 50 variables and hope something is significant. The new approach: randomly assign a program, measure the impact, and scale what works.

— The paradigm shift
🧪 Why RCTs Were Revolutionary

A Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT) is simple: randomly assign people to a program or a control group. Any difference in outcomes is the causal effect.

J-PAL (founded 2003, MIT) has run 1,000+ RCTs in 80+ countries, informing policies reaching 600M+ people.

What changed

Before: 'more aid = more growth' (maybe). After: 'this $3.50 deworming pill in Kenya increased school attendance by 25%' (definitely).

Landmark Studies

Three Studies That Changed Development Economics

🪱 Deworming in Kenya ($3.50/student)

Kremer & Miguel (2004): School-based deworming → attendance ↑25%. Cost: $3.50/student/year. 10-year follow-up: earnings ↑13%.

But: Replication controversy — some found weaker effects. The "Worm Wars" taught us: always replicate, always share data.

Lesson

Even famous results can be fragile. Context matters — worm prevalence varies. Short-run ≠ long-run effects.

📦 The Graduation Approach

Banerjee et al. (2015): For the ultra-poor, bundled intervention: asset + training + coaching + savings + health support. Tested in 6 countries.

+38%consumption at year 3
+96%asset increase
Lastingeffects persist after program ends

Why bundling works: The ultra-poor face multiple constraints at once. Cash alone doesn't work if skills are missing. Training alone doesn't work if assets are missing. The "big push" breaks all barriers simultaneously.

Cash Transfers

Just Give People Money? Yes, It Actually Works

💵 The Evidence
  • GiveDirectly (Kenya): $1,000 cash → consumption ↑27%, assets ↑58%, hunger ↓42%
  • No increase in alcohol/tobacco — the "temptation goods" myth is debunked across 19 studies
  • Mexico PROGRESA: Cash conditional on school attendance → girls' enrollment ↑9.3%
Should cash be conditional (attend school) or unconditional (no strings)?
🆚 CCT vs. UCT
Conditional (CCT)

Higher enrollment, lower dropout. Works when the goal is specific behavior change.

Unconditional (UCT)

Lower teen pregnancy, more flexibility. Respects recipient autonomy.

Vietnam Context

Vietnam reduced poverty from 58% (1993) to 5% (2020) — extraordinary. But: 20+ fragmented programs with overlapping targeting. Opportunity: consolidation into a unified digital cash transfer system.

RCT Critique

The RCT Debate: Powerful But Not Enough

⚔️ The Critics
  • Deaton (2010): RCTs tell you whether but not why. External validity is the Achilles' heel — deworming in Kenya ≠ deworming in India.
  • Rodrik (2008): You can't answer "should Vietnam pursue export-led growth?" with an RCT. Big structural questions need different tools.
  • Pritchett & Sandefur (2015): Effect sizes from RCTs often don't replicate across countries.
🤝 The Resolution: Use Everything
🎲 RCTs for...

Testing specific interventions (deworming, cash, teacher training)

📊 DiD/IV/RDD for...

Evaluating policies that can't be randomized (trade reform, minimum wage)

⚙️ Structural models for...

"What if Vietnam raised tariffs 10%?" — prediction outside sample

📈 Descriptive work for...

Establishing facts. Sometimes the most valuable contribution is simply measuring reality well.

Institutions

Do Institutions Cause Wealth? Or Does Wealth Cause Institutions?

🏛️ AJR (2001): The Famous IV

Acemoglu, Johnson & Robinson: European settlers in colonies with low mortality (US, Australia) built inclusive institutions. In high-mortality places (Congo, Nigeria), they built extractive institutions. These institutions persist and explain today's income differences.

12×income difference explained by institutions

Where European settlers could survive, they built democracies. Where they died of malaria, they built extraction machines. The ghosts of 17th-century microbes haunt today's GDP.

🗺️ Counterarguments
  • Geography (Sachs): Tropical disease and bad soil directly hurt productivity
  • Culture (Landes): Values, trust, and social capital matter
  • State capacity (Besley & Persson): It's not just the rules but the ability to implement them
Vietnam's Story

Doi Moi (1986): from central planning to market economy. Gradual reform (like China) rather than shock therapy (like Russia). The role of the state in guiding development remains central — and controversial.

Rodrik (2016): "Premature deindustrialization" — developing countries losing manufacturing jobs before getting rich. Services can't absorb labor the same way.

Can Vietnam move from assembly hub to innovation economy before the FDI-led model runs out of steam?

Nobel Prize

Nobel 2015: Angus Deaton — How Do We Even Measure Wellbeing?

🏅 Deaton's Three Big Ideas
  • Demand estimation: How do people allocate spending across goods? The "AIDS" model (1980) — used in every tax reform simulation.
  • Poverty measurement: Household surveys are essential but deeply flawed. Better measurement = better policy.
  • Preston Curve: GDP correlates with life expectancy but flattens above ~$10K. Above that, health gains come from technology and institutions, not just income.

Case & Deaton (2015) discovered that mortality among middle-aged white Americans was rising — driven by opioids, alcohol, and suicide. They called them 'deaths of despair.' This was invisible in aggregate statistics.

💊 The Big Question: Does Insurance = Health?

Two landmark experiments tested this:

RAND HIE (1971-86)

Free care ↑ usage 30% but ~0 health improvement for average person. Big effects only for low-income + chronic sick.

Oregon Lottery (2008)

Medicaid ↑ ER visits (!), ↓ depression (-30%), ↓ financial hardship. But ~0 physical health improvement in 2 years.

Surprise

Insurance protects finances and mental health, but physical health effects take longer to appear. This challenges the assumption that coverage = health.

Nobel Prize

Nobel 2014: Jean Tirole — There's No One-Size-Fits-All Regulation

🏅 Tirole's Key Insight

Markets are complex. What looks like predatory pricing in a normal market might be efficient in a platform market. Optimal regulation depends on context.

Two-sided markets: Uber, Google, Amazon serve two groups. Charging one side below cost (Google Search is free) can be welfare-enhancing, not predatory.

Google pays Apple $26 billion/year to be the default search engine. Is this an entry barrier or efficient investment? Tirole's framework says: it depends on whether it harms consumers.

41%of US market cap = VC-backed companies
6designated 'gatekeepers' under EU DMA

Key Debate

'Consumer welfare standard' (prices) vs. 'structuralist' approach (market concentration). This debate defines modern antitrust — expect it in VEO/IEO.

Competition & Innovation

Does Competition Help or Hurt Innovation?

📈 The Inverted-U

Aghion et al. (2005): The relationship is inverted-U shaped:

  • Too little competition → monopoly complacency (why innovate?)
  • Medium competition → firms innovate to "escape" rivals → maximum innovation
  • Too much competition → margins too thin to fund R&D

Some market power is good for innovation (Schumpeter was right). But monopoly is bad (antitrust authorities are also right). The optimal point is in between.

🔧 BLP: How Regulators Analyze Mergers

Berry, Levinsohn & Pakes (1995): Structural demand estimation for differentiated products. Used in every major merger review (FTC/DOJ/EC).

Steps: Estimate demand → simulate post-merger prices → predict consumer harm → approve/block.

Nobel Prize

Nobel 2018: Paul Romer — Ideas Are the Engine of Growth

🏅 The Romer Model

Before Romer: Technology falls from the sky (Solow). Growth eventually stops.
After Romer (1990): Ideas are non-rival (my use doesn't reduce yours) and respond to incentives. R&D investment drives permanent growth.

A recipe is non-rival — sharing it doesn't reduce its value. In fact, the more people who use it, the more valuable it becomes. Ideas work the same way. This is why subsidizing research has lasting effects.

— Why ideas are special
💡 Key Implications
  • More researchers → more ideas → faster growth (scale effect)
  • R&D subsidies have permanent growth effects (vs. capital subsidies which are temporary)
  • Patent protection encourages innovation but limits diffusion — need balance

Nobel Prize

Nobel 2008: Paul Krugman — Why Do France and Germany Trade Cars With Each Other?

🏅 The Puzzle

Classical trade theory says countries trade because they're different (France exports wine, Germany exports machines). But most trade is between similar countries trading similar goods.

Krugman's answer: Economies of scale + consumer love of variety. Even identical countries gain from trade because each can specialize in fewer varieties and trade for the rest.

You buy a BMW, your neighbor buys a Peugeot. Both are cars. But variety makes everyone happier — and each factory is bigger and more efficient because it serves the whole continent.

📈 From Krugman to Melitz

Melitz (2003): Not all firms export — only the most productive ones. Trade liberalization doesn't help everyone equally:

  • 🏆 Most productive firms: Export, grow, hire more
  • 🏠 Medium firms: Stay domestic
  • 💀 Least productive: Can't compete with imports → exit
Key Insight

Trade creates aggregate gains through reallocation — resources flow from less to more productive firms. But the losers are real people, not just statistics.

2.4Mmanufacturing jobs lost
$46Klifetime earnings loss per worker
↑disability claims, lower marriage rates

Adjustment was painfully slow. Workers didn't move to less-affected areas. The promise of "trade helps everyone in the long run" rang hollow.

  • Fajgelbaum et al. (2020): US-China tariffs fully passed to US consumers. Welfare loss: $51B/year. The "cure" was worse than the "disease."

Nobel Prize

Nobel 2021: The Single Most Important Question in Economics

🏅 The Credibility Revolution

The 2021 Nobel cemented a 30-year shift: economists must now show their identification strategy — explain exactly why we should believe a causal claim.

What is the counterfactual? — This is the question that defines modern empirical economics.

Before: run a regression and hope the coefficient means something. After: find a natural experiment and prove the comparison is credible.

— The paradigm shift
📐 The Rubin Framework

Each person has two potential outcomes: what happens with treatment and what happens without. We can only ever observe one. The entire field is about constructing credible comparisons to estimate the missing counterfactual.

LATE

Imbens & Angrist (1994): IV estimates the effect for compliers — those whose behavior changes because of the instrument. This is narrower than the overall average but has a credible causal interpretation.

09

The Big Five

The Five Methods Every Economist Must Know

🎲 Method Map
1. RCT

Random assignment. Gold standard. But expensive, often infeasible, and context-specific.

2. Instrumental Variables (IV)

Find an external "nudge" that affects treatment but not outcomes directly. Quarter of birth, proximity to college.

3. Difference-in-Differences (DiD)

Compare treated vs. control, before vs. after. Assumes parallel trends. NJ minimum wage (Card & Krueger).

📏 Methods 4-5
4. Regression Discontinuity (RDD)

Exploit arbitrary cutoffs (admissions scores, age thresholds). Very credible but very local.

5. Synthetic Control

Build a "fake" counterfactual from weighted comparison units. Great for case studies (one treated country/state).

When to Use What

RCT → test specific interventions. DiD → policy changes. IV → endogenous variables with external instruments. RDD → threshold-based policies. Synthetic control → single-unit case studies.

09

DiD Revolution

The Staggered DiD Problem: Why Classic Methods Were Wrong

📊 The Problem

When different units adopt a policy at different times, the classic "two-way fixed effects" regression can give wrong answers — even wrong signs.

Goodman-Bacon (2021): TWFE is a weighted average of all 2×2 comparisons. Problem: already-treated units serve as "controls" for later-treated units → biased.

If your treated group is contaminating your control group, your estimate could be completely misleading.
🔧 The New Estimators
  • Callaway & Sant'Anna (2021): Compute effects for each cohort separately, then aggregate cleanly
  • Sun & Abraham (2021): Interaction-weighted estimator for event studies
  • Causal forests (Athey & Imbens): Machine learning for heterogeneous effects — who benefits most?
Why This Matters

If you're writing an empirical paper using DiD with staggered treatment, you must use modern estimators. Reviewers will reject papers using basic TWFE.

Best Practices

How to Not Fool Yourself (or Your Audience)

✅ Checklist
  1. State the causal question clearly
  2. Identify the source of quasi-random variation
  3. Check assumptions: parallel trends, exclusion restriction, no manipulation
  4. Show event study plots before DiD estimates
  5. Report first-stage F-stat for IV (> 10)
  6. Cluster standard errors at the treatment level
  7. Pre-register when possible
⚠️ Common Traps
  • 🎣 p-hacking: Suspicious bunching of t-stats just above 1.96 in journals. Pre-registration is the antidote.
  • 📏 Pre-trends with low power: Failing to reject parallel trends ≠ parallel trends hold (Roth 2022)
  • 🏆 Winner's curse: Published effects are exaggerated by factor of 2× (Ioannidis 2017)
  • 🌍 External validity: Every method is local. LATE is for compliers; RDD is at the cutoff; RCTs are for that context.
The Master Tip

The single most important question in empirical economics: 'Compared to what?' If you can answer this for any study or policy, you understand causal inference.
