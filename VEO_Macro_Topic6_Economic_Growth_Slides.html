PART 1

What Is Growth?

Growth vs Development

Economic Growth

Quantitative: Sustained increase in real GDP or real GDP per capita.

Measured by % change in real output.

Represents outward shift of PPF or rightward shift of LRAS.

Economic Development

Qualitative: Broader improvement in living standards — health, education, equality, institutions, freedom.

Measured by HDI, Gini, life expectancy, literacy.

A country can grow without developing (e.g., oil states with inequality).

Measuring Economic Growth

$g_t = \dfrac{Y_t - Y_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}} \times 100\%$

📘 Per Capita Growth (adjusts for population)
$$g_{pc} = \frac{y_t - y_{t-1}}{y_{t-1}} \times 100\% \quad \text{where } y = Y/\text{Population}$$

If GDP grows 7% but population grows 2%, per capita growth ≈ 5%. This better measures improvement in living standards.

📘 Rule of 70 (doubling time)
$$\text{Years to double} \approx \frac{70}{g\%}$$At 7% growth, GDP doubles in ~10 years. At 2%, it takes ~35 years.

The 4 Sources of Growth

SourceDescriptionVietnam ExampleLimitation
1. Physical Capital (K)Investment in machinery, infrastructureSamsung/Intel factories; highways; portsDiminishing returns
2. Human Capital (H)Education, training, healthRising university enrolment; vocational programmesTakes decades
3. Natural Resources (N)Land, minerals, energyOil, agriculture (rice, coffee, fisheries)Can be depleted; "resource curse"
4. Technology / TFP (A)Better ways of combining inputsDigital transformation; FDI tech spilloversHardest to create but no diminishing returns!
Key insight: Only technology (TFP) can sustain long-run per-capita growth. Capital and labour face diminishing returns.

Diminishing Returns to Capital

📘 Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns
As you add more of one input (holding others constant), each additional unit contributes less and less to output.
📊 Example

A farmer with 1 tractor → huge productivity boost. 2nd tractor → still helpful. 10th tractor → barely any additional output (one farmer can only drive one tractor at a time!).

⚠️ Implication for Growth

A country cannot grow forever just by accumulating capital. At some point, each new machine adds less output. This is why technology is essential — it shifts the entire production function up.

PART 3

The Solow Model

Robert Solow (Nobel Prize 1987)

Solow Model: Key Equations

📘 Per-worker production function
$$y = f(k) \quad \text{where } y = Y/L, \; k = K/L$$Output per worker depends on capital per worker. Concave shape (diminishing returns).
📘 Capital accumulation equation
$$\Delta k = s \cdot f(k) - (n + \delta) \cdot k$$
  • $s \cdot f(k)$ = investment per worker (saving rate × output)
  • $(n+\delta) \cdot k$ = "break-even" investment (replace depreciation + equip new workers)
  • $s$ = saving rate, $n$ = population growth, $\delta$ = depreciation rate

Solow Model: The Steady State

Steady State: $s \cdot f(k^*) = (n + \delta) \cdot k^*$

Investment exactly replaces depreciation + new workers → k stops changing

At steady state:

  • Capital per worker constant
  • Output per worker constant
  • No per-capita growth (without technology!)
  • Total GDP grows at rate $n$ (population growth only)

What changes the steady state?

  • Higher $s$ → higher $k^*$ → higher $y^*$ (richer steady state)
  • Higher $n$ or $\delta$ → lower $k^*$ → poorer steady state
  • But none of these change the long-run growth rate!

Solow: The Role of Saving

Higher saving rate → more investment → more capital accumulation → higher level of income (richer steady state).
⚠️ But NOT higher long-run growth rate!

After the transition to the new steady state, per-capita growth returns to zero (without technology). Saving affects the level, not the growth rate.

💡 Golden Rule
The saving rate that maximises steady-state consumption (not output) per worker: $MPK = n + \delta$. Too much saving → too much investment, not enough consumption.

Solow: Technology as the Growth Engine

Only technological progress (growth in A) drives sustained per-capita growth in the long run.

With technology growing at rate $g$:$$\text{Per-capita GDP growth rate} = g \text{ (in the long run)}$$Technology shifts the entire production function upward — each unit of capital produces more. No diminishing returns to ideas!
📊 Summary of Long-Run Growth Rates (Solow)
VariableGrowth Rate
Output per worker (y)$g$ (tech growth rate)
Capital per worker (k)$g$
Total output (Y)$n + g$
Total capital (K)$n + g$

Convergence Hypothesis

Conditional Convergence ✓

Countries with similar institutions, saving rates, and policies converge to the same steady state.

Poorer countries (further from steady state) grow faster — capital has higher marginal product.

Evidence supports this.

Unconditional Convergence ✗

ALL countries converge regardless of institutions.

NOT supported. Many poor countries remain poor due to weak institutions, corruption, conflict.

Institutions matter enormously!

PART 4 — VEO KEY TOPIC

The Middle-Income Trap

What is the Middle-Income Trap?

📘 Definition (World Bank, 2007)
A situation where countries that reached middle-income status stall and fail to progress to high-income. Strategies that drove growth from low → middle income (cheap labour, capital accumulation) face diminishing returns and stop working.

Why countries get stuck:

  • Diminishing returns to factor accumulation (Solow!)
  • Can't compete with low-income countries on labour costs anymore
  • Can't compete with high-income countries on technology/innovation yet
  • Weak institutions, corruption, insufficient R&D
  • Education system produces quantity, not quality
  • "Premature deindustrialisation"

SWOT: Vietnam Escaping the Trap

💪 Strengths

  • Sustained 6–7% growth
  • Young, large labour force
  • Political stability
  • Strategic location in SE Asia
  • Successful reform track record (Đổi Mới)

⚡ Weaknesses

  • Low labour productivity vs peers
  • Over-reliance on FDI
  • Education-industry mismatch
  • Infrastructure gaps
  • Institutional weaknesses

🌟 Opportunities

  • China+1 supply chain shift
  • Digital transformation / Industry 4.0
  • Multiple FTAs (CPTPP, EVFTA, RCEP)
  • Growing middle class
  • Green transition potential

⚠️ Threats

  • Ageing population approaching
  • Climate change vulnerability
  • Geopolitical tensions (South China Sea)
  • Competition from Bangladesh, Cambodia
  • Global slowdowns
