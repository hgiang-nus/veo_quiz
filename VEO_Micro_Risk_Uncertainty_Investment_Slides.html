PART 1

Describing Risk

How do we measure and compare risky outcomes?

Probability & Expected Value

📘 Probability
The likelihood that a given outcome will occur. Values between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain). All probabilities must sum to 1.
📘 Expected Value (EV)
$$E(X) = Pr_1 \cdot X_1 + Pr_2 \cdot X_2 + \ldots + Pr_n \cdot X_n$$ The probability-weighted average of all possible payoffs. It measures the "central tendency" — what you'd get on average if you repeated the gamble many times.
📊 Example
A stock has a 1/4 chance of reaching $40 and a 3/4 chance of falling to $20:
$E(X) = (0.25)(40) + (0.75)(20) = 10 + 15 = $ $25

Variability: How Spread Out Are Outcomes?

Two gambles can have the same expected value but very different riskiness. We need a measure of spread.
Outcome 1ProbOutcome 2ProbEV
Job 1 (Commission)$2,0000.5$1,0000.5$1,500
Job 2 (Fixed Salary)$1,5100.99$5100.01$1,500

Both jobs pay $1,500 on average. But Job 1 has much wider swings. Which is riskier?

We measure risk using variance (average squared deviation from the mean) and standard deviation (square root of variance).

📊 Computing Standard Deviation

📘 Formula
$$\sigma = \sqrt{\sum_{i} Pr_i \cdot (X_i - E(X))^2}$$
Deviation²₁Deviation²₂VarianceStd Dev (σ)
Job 1(2000−1500)²=250,000(1000−1500)²=250,000.5(250k)+.5(250k)=250,000$500
Job 2(1510−1500)²=100(510−1500)²=980,100.99(100)+.01(980,100)=9,900$99.50
Job 1: σ = $500. Job 2: σ = $99.50. Job 2 is much less risky (lower σ) even though both have the same EV of $1,500.

Decision Making Under Risk

What if two options have different expected values AND different risk?

Expected IncomeStd Deviation
Job 1 (modified)$1,600$500

Choose Job 1 if...

You're willing to accept more risk for a higher expected return. Aggressive entrepreneurs might prefer this.

Choose Job 2 if...

You prefer certainty and are uncomfortable with large income swings. Conservative people prefer this.
Your choice depends on your preferences toward risk — which brings us to the next section.

PART 2

Preferences Toward Risk

Are you risk averse, risk loving, or risk neutral?

Expected Utility

📘 Key Insight
People don't maximise expected money — they maximise expected utility. Because utility is concave (diminishing marginal utility of income), a guaranteed $20,000 may give MORE utility than a 50-50 gamble between $10,000 and $30,000.
📘 Expected Utility Formula
$$E(u) = Pr_1 \cdot u(X_1) + Pr_2 \cdot u(X_2) + \ldots + Pr_n \cdot u(X_n)$$
📊 Example
Income = $15,000 currently (u = 13.5). New risky job: 50% chance of $10,000 (u=10) or $30,000 (u=18).
$E(u) = 0.5(10) + 0.5(18) = 14 > 13.5$ → Take the risky job! (Higher expected utility)

Three Types of Risk Preferences

😰

Risk Averse

Prefers a certain income to a risky one with the same EV.

Utility function: concave (diminishing MU of income)

u(E(X)) > E(u(X))

Most people are risk averse.

🎰

Risk Loving

Prefers the gamble to a certain income with the same EV.

Utility function: convex (increasing MU)

u(E(X)) < E(u(X))

Gamblers, extreme risk-takers.

😐

Risk Neutral

Indifferent between gamble and certain income with the same EV.

Utility function: linear

u(E(X)) = E(u(X))

Only cares about EV.

Why Concavity → Risk Aversion

📘 Jensen's Inequality
If $u$ is concave (diminishing MU): $$u(E(X)) > E(u(X))$$ The utility of the expected value exceeds the expected utility. This means a guaranteed amount gives more utility than the risky gamble with the same average.
📊 Numerical Example

Gamble: 50% chance of $10,000 (u=10) and 50% chance of $30,000 (u=18).

$E(X) = 0.5(10{,}000) + 0.5(30{,}000) = \$20{,}000$

$u(E(X)) = u(20{,}000) = 16$

$E(u(X)) = 0.5(10) + 0.5(18) = 14$

$16 > 14$ → The certain $20,000 gives higher utility → risk averse person prefers the sure thing.

The Risk Premium

📘 Definition
The risk premium = the maximum amount a risk-averse person would pay to avoid a gamble and receive a certain outcome instead.
📊 Example (from Pindyck/Rubinfeld)

Gamble: 50% of $10,000, 50% of $30,000. EV = $20,000. Expected utility = 14.

A certain income of $16,000 also gives utility = 14.

Risk premium = $20,000 − $16,000 = $4,000

The person would accept $4,000 less than the EV just to avoid the uncertainty!

Higher risk aversion → larger risk premium. More concave utility → person gives up more money for certainty.

🏆 VEO Application: Insurance

Michael Schumacher's house is worth $50,000. Probability of burglary = 10%, losing $10,000. Should he buy insurance for $1,000?

Analysis:

Burglary (p=0.1)No Burglary (p=0.9)E(Wealth)σ
No insurance$40,000$50,000$49,000$3,000
With insurance$49,000$49,000$49,000$0

Both options give the same expected wealth ($49,000). But with insurance: zero risk. A risk-averse person always prefers insurance when the premium equals the expected loss (= actuarially fair).

Ways to Reduce Risk

🔀

1. Diversification

Spread resources across activities whose outcomes are not closely correlated.

Sell both ACs and heaters → guaranteed income regardless of weather!

🛡️

2. Insurance

Pay a premium to transfer risk to an insurance company. Works because of the law of large numbers.

📊

3. Information

More info → better decisions → less risk. The value of information = E(with info) − E(without info).

📊 Diversification: Worked Example

Hot Weather (p=0.5)Cold Weather (p=0.5)
AC sales only$30,000$12,000
Heater sales only$12,000$30,000
50/50 both (diversified)$15,000 + $6,000 = $21,000$6,000 + $15,000 = $21,000

Without diversification: EV = $21,000 but income varies ($12K or $30K).

With diversification: Income = $21,000 for certain. Risk completely eliminated!

This works because AC and heater sales are negatively correlated — when one goes up, the other goes down.

Stock market application: Mutual funds diversify across many stocks → reduces risk without reducing expected return.

📊 The Value of Information

Value of complete information = Expected payoff with perfect info − Expected payoff under uncertainty
Sales = 50 (p=0.5)Sales = 100 (p=0.5)E(Profit)
Buy 50 suits$5,000$5,000$5,000
Buy 100 suits$1,500$12,000$6,750

Best without info: buy 100 suits → E = $6,750

With perfect info: if sales=50→buy 50→$5,000; if sales=100→buy 100→$12,000

E(with info) = 0.5(5,000) + 0.5(12,000) = $8,500

Value of information = $8,500 − $6,750 = $1,750

PART 3

The Time Value
of Money

A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow

Present Discounted Value (PDV)

📘 Core Idea
Money received in the future is worth less than money today — because money today can be invested to earn interest.

$PDV = \dfrac{\$1}{(1+R)^n}$

$R$ = interest rate, $n$ = years from now

📊 Example

$1 received 10 years from now at R = 5%:

$PDV = 1/(1.05)^{10} = 1/1.629 = $ $0.614

$1 in 10 years is only worth 61 cents today!

Two key patterns: (1) Higher interest rates → lower PDV (future money worth less). (2) Longer time → lower PDV (more discounting). At 10%, $1 in 30 years is worth only 5.7 cents today!

The Discount Factor δ

$\delta = \dfrac{1}{1+R}$ where $0 < \delta < 1$

The discount factor makes calculations cleaner. Instead of $\frac{1}{(1+R)^n}$, we write $\delta^n$.

📘 Useful Summation Formulas
$\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \delta^t = \frac{1}{1-\delta}$Infinite series starting now
$\sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \delta^t = \frac{\delta}{1-\delta} = \frac{1}{R}$Infinite series starting next period
$\sum_{t=1}^{y} \delta^t = \frac{\delta - \delta^{y+1}}{1-\delta}$Finite series (y periods)

The Value of a Bond

📘 Bond
A contract where a borrower pays the lender (bondholder) a stream of money: annual coupon payments plus the principal (face value) at maturity.
📘 Bond PDV Formula

A bond pays coupon $C$ each year for $T$ years, then repays principal $F$:

$$PDV = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{C}{(1+R)^t} + \frac{F}{(1+R)^T}$$
📊 Example
10-year bond, coupon = $100/year, principal = $1,000. At R=5%: PDV ≈ $1,386. At R=15%: PDV ≈ $749.
Higher interest rates → lower bond prices.

Perpetuities

📘 Perpetuity
A bond that pays a fixed amount forever. No maturity date.

$PDV = \dfrac{C}{R}$

C = annual payment, R = interest rate

A perpetuity paying $100/year:

  • At R = 5%: PDV = 100/0.05 = $2,000
  • At R = 10%: PDV = 100/0.10 = $1,000
  • At R = 20%: PDV = 100/0.20 = $500
📘 Effective Yield
If a perpetuity paying $100/year sells for $1,000, the interest rate (effective yield) is: $R = C/P = 100/1000 = 10\%$

🏆 Practice: Bond Valuation

A government bond pays $50/year for 5 years, then returns the $1,000 face value. Interest rate = 8%. What is it worth today?

Solution:

$PDV = \frac{50}{1.08} + \frac{50}{1.08^2} + \frac{50}{1.08^3} + \frac{50}{1.08^4} + \frac{50}{1.08^5} + \frac{1000}{1.08^5}$

$= 46.30 + 42.87 + 39.69 + 36.75 + 34.03 + 680.58$

$= $ $880.22

The bond trades below face value ($1,000) because the coupon rate (5%) is less than the market rate (8%).

PART 4

Investment Decisions
& NPV

Net Present Value (NPV)

📘 NPV Criterion
Invest if and only if the present value of future cash flows exceeds the cost.

$NPV = -C + \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{\pi_t}{(1+R)^t}$

C = initial cost, $\pi_t$ = profit in year t, R = discount rate

NPV > 0

✅ INVEST

Project creates value

NPV < 0

❌ DON'T INVEST

Project destroys value

📊 NPV: Electric Motor Factory

Cost: $10 million today. Annual profit: $960,000 for 20 years. Scrap value: $1 million at year 20.

$$NPV = -10{,}000{,}000 + \sum_{t=1}^{20} \frac{960{,}000}{(1+R)^t} + \frac{1{,}000{,}000}{(1+R)^{20}}$$
Discount Rate RNPVDecision
3%+$4.72MINVEST ✓
5%+$2.41MINVEST ✓
7.5% (R*)≈ $0BREAK-EVEN
10%−$1.56MDON'T INVEST ✗
15%−$3.93MDON'T INVEST ✗
R* = 7.5% is the Internal Rate of Return (IRR) — the discount rate where NPV = 0. Invest if your opportunity cost < IRR.

Real vs Nominal Discount Rates

If future cash flows are in real terms (adjusted for inflation), use the real discount rate.

$$R_{real} \approx R_{nominal} - \pi$$

$\pi$ = expected inflation rate

📊 Example

Nominal interest rate on government bonds = 9%. Expected inflation = 5%.

Real rate = 9% − 5% = 4%

Use 4% to discount the motor factory's real cash flows. At 4%, NPV is clearly positive → invest!

Common VEO mistake: Using nominal rates to discount real cash flows (or vice versa). Always match: real with real, nominal with nominal.

Adjusting NPV for Risk

Diversifiable Risk

Can be eliminated by investing in many projects or holding many stocks.

No risk premium needed. Use the risk-free rate.

Non-diversifiable Risk

Cannot be eliminated — tied to overall economy.

Requires a risk premium. Use a higher discount rate.

📘 CAPM (Capital Asset Pricing Model)
$$r_i = r_f + \beta_i (r_m - r_f)$$
  • $r_f$ = risk-free rate
  • $r_m$ = expected market return
  • $\beta_i$ = asset beta (sensitivity to market)
  • $(r_m - r_f)$ = market risk premium

🏆 Practice: CAPM & Discount Rate

A firm's investment has β = 1.5. Risk-free rate = 4%. Market risk premium = 8%. What discount rate should the firm use?

$r_i = r_f + \beta(r_m - r_f) = 4\% + 1.5 \times 8\% = 4\% + 12\% = $ 16%

This is the risk-adjusted discount rate. Use it in the NPV formula. A riskier project (higher β) requires a higher rate → harder to justify (lower NPV).

📘 Interpretation of β
  • $\beta = 1$: Project moves with the market (average risk)
  • $\beta > 1$: More volatile than market (riskier → higher required return)
  • $\beta < 1$: Less volatile (safer → lower required return)
  • $\beta = 0$: Uncorrelated with market → use risk-free rate

Investment Decisions by Consumers

Consumers face investment decisions too — buying durable goods (cars, appliances) involves comparing upfront cost vs future benefits.
📊 Buying a Car
$$NPV = -\text{Price} + \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{S - E}{(1+R)^t} + \frac{\text{Resale}}{(1+R)^T}$$

$S$ = value of driving service per year, $E$ = operating expenses per year.

Buy if NPV > 0. A high discount rate makes future benefits less valuable → less likely to buy an expensive car.

📊 Choosing an Appliance

Cheap AC: low price, high operating cost. Expensive AC: high price, low operating cost.

Compare $PDV = C_i + OC_i \times \sum_{t=1}^{8}\delta^t$ for each. Low discount rate → efficient (expensive) AC wins.

Investment in Human Capital

📘 Human Capital
Knowledge, skills, and experience that make a person more productive → earn higher income over a lifetime. Education is an investment!
📊 NPV of a College Degree

Cost: $40,000/year × 4 years. Benefit: $20,000/year extra salary for 20 years after graduation.

$$NPV = -\sum_{t=0}^{3}\frac{40{,}000}{(1+R)^t} + \sum_{t=4}^{23}\frac{20{,}000}{(1+R)^t}$$

At R = 5%: NPV ≈ $66,000 → College is a very good investment!

At R = 15%: NPV turns negative → Not worth it at very high discount rates.

🏆 Practice: NPV Calculation

A Vietnamese coffee farm costs 500 million VND to set up. It generates 80 million VND/year for 10 years, then the land is sold for 200 million VND. Discount rate = 8%. Should the farmer invest?

Solution:

$NPV = -500 + 80 \times \frac{\delta - \delta^{11}}{1-\delta} + 200 \times \delta^{10}$ where $\delta = 1/1.08$

$= -500 + 80 \times 6.710 + 200 \times 0.4632$

$= -500 + 536.8 + 92.6 = $ +129.4 million VND

NPV > 0 → Invest!

Behavioral Economics

When people don't behave "rationally"

Behavioral Economics: Key Concepts

Standard theory assumes rational utility maximisation. But real people show systematic biases:

ConceptDescriptionExample
Endowment effectValue items more when you own themHomeowners set asking price too high
Loss aversionLosses hurt more than equivalent gains feel goodPeople hold losing stocks too long
FramingDecisions depend on how choices are presented"10% fat" vs "90% fat-free"
AnchoringRely too heavily on initial informationFirst price seen affects willingness to pay
Law of small numbersOverstate probabilities from small samples"Stock rose 3 years → will keep rising"

Bubbles & Informational Cascades

📘 Bubble
A price increase driven NOT by fundamentals, but by the belief that prices will keep rising.

Internet Bubble (2000)

Investors paid extreme prices for dot-com companies with no profits. NASDAQ fell 78% from peak.

US Housing Bubble (2008)

Housing prices soared as people believed "prices only go up." Banks lent recklessly. Prices fell 28%+ → Great Recession.

📘 Informational Cascade
People follow others' actions rather than their own information. If early investors buy → later investors assume it's good → more buying → bubble. Rational individually, but fragile collectively.

🏆 VEO Application: Deterring Crime

A city wants to deter speeding. It can: (a) fine $5 and catch every violator, or (b) fine $50 and catch 1 in 10, or (c) fine $500 and catch 1 in 100. Which is most efficient?

Expected fine in all cases = $5:

  • (a) $5 × 1.0 = $5
  • (b) $50 × 0.1 = $5
  • (c) $500 × 0.01 = $5

All have the same expected penalty. But (c) has the lowest enforcement cost — catching 1 in 100 is much cheaper than catching every violator.

This works especially well if drivers are risk averse — they fear the large fine more than a risk-neutral calculation would suggest.

🏆 VEO 2021: Insurance & Expected Utility

Michael Schumacher earns $100,000. He faces a 25% risk of injury → income drops to $10,000. Utility function: $u(I) = \sqrt{I}$.

(a) Expected income? (b) Expected utility? (c) Should he buy insurance at premium $20,000?

(a) $E(I) = 0.75(100{,}000) + 0.25(10{,}000) = $ $77,500

(b) $E(u) = 0.75\sqrt{100{,}000} + 0.25\sqrt{10{,}000} = 0.75(316.23) + 0.25(100) = 237.17 + 25 = $ 262.17

(c) With insurance: certain income = $100,000 − $20,000 = $80,000. $u(80{,}000) = \sqrt{80{,}000} = $ 282.84

282.84 > 262.17 → Buy insurance! (certain utility > expected utility of gamble)

🏆 Practice: Computing Risk Premium

Using the Schumacher example above: What is the risk premium? (i.e., how much would he pay above the actuarially fair premium?)

Actuarially fair premium = expected loss = 0.25 × $90,000 = $22,500

With fair insurance: certain income = $100,000 − $22,500 = $77,500. $u = \sqrt{77{,}500} = 278.39$

This is still > 262.17 (no insurance) → would buy even at the fair premium.

Find the certainty equivalent: solve $\sqrt{CE} = 262.17$

$CE = (262.17)^2 = $ $68,733

Risk premium = E(I) − CE = $77,500 − $68,733 = $8,767

He'd accept $8,767 less than the EV just for certainty.

Bonus: Depletable Resources & Time

📘 The Hotelling Rule
For exhaustible resources (oil, gas, minerals), the price minus extraction cost must rise at the rate of interest: $$\frac{(P_{t+1} - c) - (P_t - c)}{P_t - c} = R$$

Intuition

Keeping oil in the ground is like an investment. If $P-c$ rises slower than R, it's better to extract now and invest the money. If it rises faster, wait.

In equilibrium: indifferent between extracting now or later.

Monopolist

A monopolist depletes the resource more slowly than a competitive industry. MR − c rises at rate R, but since MR < P, the price rises slower than R.

Monopolists are more "conservationist"!

⚠️ Common Exam Mistakes

❌ Don't
  • Confuse expected value with expected utility
  • Forget to square deviations when computing variance
  • Mix up nominal and real discount rates
  • Use IRR instead of NPV for comparing projects of different sizes
  • Say "risk averse = avoids all risk" (they just need compensation)
  • Forget that bond prices move inversely with interest rates
✅ Do
  • Always state whether computing EV or E(u)
  • Show the utility function shape (concave = risk averse)
  • Use real rates for real cash flows
  • Add risk premium to discount rate for risky projects
  • Remember: diversification reduces risk WITHOUT reducing expected return
