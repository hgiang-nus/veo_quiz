Introduction to Macroeconomics

What macroeconomists study, how they think, and why it matters

What is Macroeconomics?

📘 Definition
Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole — aggregate output, unemployment, inflation, interest rates, exchange rates, and the effects of government policy on these variables.

Contrast with Microeconomics: studies individual households, firms, and markets.

Key Questions Macroeconomists Ask

  • Why does the cost of living keep rising? (Inflation)
  • Why are millions unemployed even in good times? (Unemployment)
  • Why do economies experience booms and busts? (Business cycles)
  • Why are some countries rich and others poor? (Economic growth)
  • What should the government do about recessions? (Fiscal & monetary policy)
  • Why do countries trade? Who benefits? (International trade)
🏆 VEO 2021 Practice
Q: Which content does Macroeconomics study?
(a) Variables of the whole economy   (b) Consumers' choices between fish and beef   (c) A strike affecting one company   (d) Government support for lychee price
Answer: (a) — (b), (c), (d) are microeconomics topics (individual markets/firms).

Models: How Economists Think

📘 Key Terms
  • Endogenous variables: Variables the model tries to explain (outputs).
  • Exogenous variables: Variables the model takes as given (inputs).
  • Market clearing: Prices adjust so supply = demand (equilibrium).
  • Sticky prices/wages: In reality, prices don't adjust instantly — important for short-run macro.
📊 The Macroeconomic System
Inputs (exogenous): weather, politics, population, technology, policy choices
→ Black Box: Aggregate Supply + Aggregate Demand interaction
→ Outputs (endogenous): GDP, employment, price level, inflation, interest rates, trade balance

The Data of Macroeconomics

GDP, GNP, CPI — measuring the economy

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

📘 Definition
GDP = the market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period of time.

Rules for Computing GDP

RuleExplanation
Use market pricesGDP = Σ (Price × Quantity) for all final goods
Final goods onlyIntermediate goods excluded (already in final price). Use value added to avoid double-counting.
No used goodsOnly count production in current period
InventoriesGoods produced but not sold = inventory investment (counted in GDP when produced)
Imputed valuesNon-market goods (e.g., owner-occupied housing) estimated at market equivalent

Three Methods of Computing GDP

📊 The Three Approaches (all give the same answer)
1. Expenditure Approach: $$Y = C + I + G + NX$$ where $C$ = consumption, $I$ = investment, $G$ = government purchases, $NX$ = net exports (exports − imports). 2. Income Approach: $$GDP = W + R + i + \Pi + D + T_e$$ where $W$ = wages, $R$ = rent, $i$ = interest, $\Pi$ = profit, $D$ = depreciation, $T_e$ = indirect taxes. 3. Output (Value Added) Approach: $$GDP = \sum \text{Value Added in all industries}$$ Value Added = Revenue − Cost of intermediate inputs.

Nominal vs Real GDP

📘 Key Distinction
  • Nominal GDP: Measured at current prices. Changes when either prices or quantities change.
  • Real GDP: Measured at constant (base-year) prices. Changes only when quantities change.
  • GDP Deflator $= \dfrac{\text{Nominal GDP}}{\text{Real GDP}} \times 100$ — measures the overall price level.
🏆 VEO 2021 Practice
Q: Nominal GDP rose from 3,421 to 3,521 USD; GDP Deflator rose from 262 to 270. Find % changes.
Answer: Nominal GDP change = $(3521-3421)/3421 \times 100 = 2.92\%$. Deflator change = $(270-262)/262 \times 100 = 3.05\%$. Real GDP change ≈ $2.92\% - 3.05\% = -0.13\%$ (real GDP fell slightly despite nominal rise — prices grew faster than output).

GNP vs GDP

$$\text{GNP} = \text{GDP} + \text{Net Income from Abroad}$$ GDP = production within borders. GNP = production by domestic residents (anywhere).

Consumer Price Index (CPI)

📘 CPI
$$CPI_t = \frac{\sum P_t^i \cdot Q_0^i}{\sum P_0^i \cdot Q_0^i} \times 100$$ CPI measures the cost of a fixed basket of goods over time (Laspeyres index). It tracks the cost of living.
💡 CPI vs GDP Deflator
CPIGDP Deflator
BasketFixed (base year)Changes with current output
Goods includedConsumer goods only (incl. imports)All domestically produced goods
BiasOverstates inflation (substitution bias)Understates inflation

Aggregate Demand & Fiscal Policy

Keynesian economics: how spending determines output

Aggregate Demand (AD)

📘 Definition
Aggregate Demand (AD) = total spending on goods and services in the economy at each price level. $$AD = C + I + G + (X - M)$$ The AD curve slopes downward: higher price level → lower real spending (wealth effect, interest rate effect, exchange rate effect).

Shifts in AD

AD shifts right (increase) when:

  • Consumer confidence rises → $C \uparrow$
  • Business investment increases → $I \uparrow$
  • Government spending increases → $G \uparrow$
  • Net exports rise (foreign demand up, or currency depreciates) → $NX \uparrow$

AD shifts left (decrease) when the opposite occurs.

Aggregate Supply (AS)

📘 Short-Run vs Long-Run AS
  • SRAS (Short-Run AS): Upward-sloping. Higher prices → firms produce more (sticky wages/contracts).
  • LRAS (Long-Run AS): Vertical at potential output $Y^*$. In the long run, output depends on real factors (technology, capital, labour), not the price level.

Fiscal Policy

📘 Definition
Fiscal policy = government use of taxation and government spending to influence aggregate demand.
ExpansionaryContractionary
GoalStimulate economy (fight recession)Cool economy (fight inflation)
SpendingIncrease $G$Decrease $G$
TaxesCut taxes → $C \uparrow$Raise taxes → $C \downarrow$
AD effectAD shifts rightAD shifts left
📊 The Multiplier Effect
An increase in government spending of $\Delta G$ leads to a larger increase in GDP: $$\Delta Y = \frac{1}{1 - MPC} \times \Delta G$$ where $MPC$ = marginal propensity to consume. If $MPC = 0.8$, the multiplier = $1/(1-0.8) = 5$.

Tax multiplier: $\Delta Y = -\frac{MPC}{1-MPC} \times \Delta T$ (smaller than spending multiplier because some of the tax cut is saved).

Money and Monetary Policy

Functions of money, central banking, and money creation

What is Money?

📘 Three Functions of Money
  1. Medium of exchange: Accepted for buying/selling goods and services.
  2. Unit of account: Common measure to state prices and compare values.
  3. Store of value: Can be held and exchanged later for goods/services.
🏆 VEO 2021 Practice
Q: When estimating total learning costs for next year, which function of money do you apply?
Answer: (c) Unit of account — you're using money as a measuring stick to evaluate/compare costs. You're not exchanging or storing it.

Money Creation by Commercial Banks

📊 The Money Multiplier
With fractional-reserve banking (reserve ratio $rr$): $$\text{Money Multiplier} = \frac{1}{rr}$$ $$\text{Total Money Created} = \text{Initial Deposit} \times \frac{1}{rr}$$ Example: Reserve ratio = 25%. New deposit = 3M VND. Total new money = $3M / 0.25 = 12M$ VND.

Central Bank Tools

ToolHow it worksExpansionaryContractionary
Reserve RequirementsMin reserves banks must holdLower $rr$ → more lending → MS↑Raise $rr$ → less lending → MS↓
Discount RateRate CB charges banks for loansLower rate → banks borrow more → MS↑Raise rate → banks borrow less → MS↓
Open Market OperationsBuy/sell government bondsBuy bonds → inject money → MS↑Sell bonds → absorb money → MS↓
🔑 Monetary Policy Summary
Expansionary: Increase money supply → lower interest rates → more borrowing/spending → AD↑
Contractionary: Decrease money supply → higher interest rates → less borrowing/spending → AD↓

Inflation and Unemployment

CPI, causes of inflation, types of unemployment, the Phillips Curve

Unemployment

📊 Key Formulas
$$\text{Labour Force} = \text{Employed} + \text{Unemployed}$$ $$\text{Adult Population} = \text{Labour Force} + \text{Not in Labour Force}$$ $$\text{Unemployment Rate} = \frac{U}{L} \times 100\%$$ $$\text{Labour Force Participation Rate} = \frac{L}{\text{Adult Population}} \times 100\%$$

Types of Unemployment

TypeCauseDuration
FrictionalWorkers between jobs; information imperfectShort-term, natural
StructuralSkills mismatch; economy restructuringLonger-term, natural
CyclicalRecession; AD below full-employment levelVaries with business cycle
ClassicalWage set above equilibrium (e.g., minimum wage)Persistent
📘 Natural Rate of Unemployment
$u^* = $ frictional + structural unemployment. This is the "normal" unemployment rate even when the economy is at full employment. Cyclical unemployment = 0 when $u = u^*$.

Inflation

📊 Computing Inflation
$$\text{Inflation Rate}_t = \frac{CPI_t - CPI_{t-1}}{CPI_{t-1}} \times 100\%$$

Causes of Inflation

TypeMechanismAD-AS
Demand-pullExcess spending → "too much money chasing too few goods"AD shifts right
Cost-pushRising input costs (wages, oil, materials) push prices upSRAS shifts left
StructuralDemand shifts between sectors; prices rise in expanding sectors but don't fall in contracting ones—
ExpectationsIf people expect inflation, they demand higher wages → self-fulfillingSRAS shifts left

The Phillips Curve

📘 Short-Run Phillips Curve
Short-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment: lower unemployment → higher inflation, and vice versa. $$\pi = \pi^e - \beta(u - u^*)$$ where $\pi$ = inflation, $\pi^e$ = expected inflation, $u$ = unemployment, $u^*$ = natural rate.

Long-run: The Phillips Curve is vertical at $u^*$. In the long run, there is no trade-off — inflation is determined by monetary policy, unemployment settles at the natural rate.

Economic Growth

⭐ EXPANDED — Sources of growth, Solow model, convergence, VEO applications

What is Economic Growth?

📘 Definition
Economic growth = sustained increase in potential output (real GDP) over time. It represents an outward shift of the Production Possibility Frontier (PPF) or a rightward shift of LRAS.

Economic growth ≠ Economic development. Growth is quantitative (more output). Development is broader: improved living standards, health, education, institutions, equality.
📊 Measuring Growth
$$g_t = \frac{Y_t - Y_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}} \times 100\%$$ Per capita growth (adjusts for population): $$g_{pc,t} = \frac{y_t - y_{t-1}}{y_{t-1}} \times 100\% \qquad \text{where } y = Y/\text{Population}$$

Sources of Economic Growth

The Production Function

📊 Aggregate Production Function
$$Y = A \cdot F(K, L, H, N)$$
  • $Y$ = total output (real GDP)
  • $A$ = total factor productivity (TFP) — technology, efficiency, institutions
  • $K$ = physical capital (machines, factories, infrastructure)
  • $L$ = labour (number of workers)
  • $H$ = human capital (education, skills, health of workers)
  • $N$ = natural resources (land, minerals, energy)

The Four Sources of Growth

SourceDescriptionVietnam Example
1. Physical Capital (K)Investment in machinery, infrastructure, factories. Subject to diminishing returns.FDI inflows into manufacturing (Samsung, Intel); highway/port construction
2. Human Capital (H)Education, training, health improvements → more productive workers.Rising university enrolment; vocational training programmes
3. Natural Resources (N)Land, minerals, energy. Can be depleted. Not sufficient alone.Oil, gas, agriculture (rice, coffee, fisheries)
4. Technology / TFP (A)Better ways of combining inputs. The only source of sustained long-run growth (no diminishing returns).Digital transformation; tech adoption from FDI spillovers
🏆 VEO 2021 Practice
Q: What variable plays the most significant role in accounting for huge differences in living standards worldwide?
Answer: (a) Productivity — output per worker. Countries are rich because their workers are productive, driven by capital, human capital, and technology.

The Solow Growth Model

📘 Key Ideas of the Solow Model
  1. Capital accumulation alone cannot sustain growth due to diminishing returns to capital.
  2. Steady state: The economy converges to a point where investment = depreciation. At the steady state, capital per worker and output per worker are constant (no growth in per-capita terms).
  3. Only technological progress drives sustained per-capita growth in the long run.
  4. Saving rate affects the level of income (higher saving → more capital → higher steady-state income) but not the long-run growth rate.
📊 Solow Model Equations (per worker)
Production function per worker: $y = f(k)$ where $y = Y/L$, $k = K/L$.

Capital accumulation: $\Delta k = s \cdot f(k) - (n + \delta) \cdot k$
  • $s$ = saving rate
  • $n$ = population growth rate
  • $\delta$ = depreciation rate
Steady state: $\Delta k = 0 \Rightarrow s \cdot f(k^*) = (n + \delta) \cdot k^*$

Convergence Hypothesis

💡 Do poor countries catch up?
Conditional convergence: Countries with similar institutions, saving rates, and policies converge to the same steady state. Poorer countries (further from their steady state) grow faster because capital has higher marginal product.

Unconditional convergence (all countries converge regardless) is NOT supported by evidence — institutions and policies matter enormously.

The Middle-Income Trap

⚠️ VEO Key Topic
Countries that grew rapidly using cheap labour and capital accumulation stall at middle-income because:
  • Diminishing returns to factor accumulation
  • Failure to transition from factor-driven to innovation-driven growth
  • Weak institutions, insufficient R&D, education misalignment
Vietnam context: GNI per capita ~$4,180 (2023). Target: high-income by 2045. Needs to shift from labour-intensive manufacturing to technology, innovation, and high-value services.

Policies to Promote Growth

International Trade & The Open Economy

⭐ EXPANDED — Comparative advantage, trade policy, exchange rates, balance of payments

Why Do Countries Trade?

Absolute vs Comparative Advantage

📘 Key Definitions
  • Absolute advantage: A country can produce a good using fewer resources than another country.
  • Comparative advantage: A country can produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another country.
Trade is based on comparative advantage, not absolute advantage. Even if one country is better at producing everything, both countries benefit from specialising in what they do relatively best.
📊 How to Find Comparative Advantage
Example (from VEO Practice):
US: 150 wheat OR 50 steel.    Vietnam: 40 wheat OR 20 steel.
OC of 1 SteelOC of 1 Wheat
US150/50 = 3 wheat50/150 = 1/3 steel
Vietnam40/20 = 2 wheat ✓ lower20/40 = 1/2 steel
Vietnam: comparative advantage in steel (lower OC: 2 < 3).
US: comparative advantage in wheat (lower OC: 1/3 < 1/2).
Both gain from trade if Vietnam specialises in steel and US in wheat.

Gains from Trade

💡 Why trade benefits both countries
  1. Specialisation: Each country produces what it's relatively best at → total world output increases.
  2. Consumption beyond the PPF: Through trade, countries can consume combinations of goods beyond what they could produce alone.
  3. Terms of Trade: The exchange ratio must lie between the two countries' opportunity costs for both to gain. In the example: 1 steel trades for between 2 and 3 wheat.

🔑 Vietnam's Trade Integration
Vietnam is one of the most open economies in the world:
  • Trade/GDP ratio: ~200% (among highest globally)
  • Key FTAs: CPTPP, EVFTA (EU), RCEP, ASEAN, bilateral agreements
  • Export structure: Electronics (Samsung), textiles/garments, seafood, agricultural products
  • Import structure: Machinery, raw materials, electronics components
  • Major beneficiary of China+1 supply chain diversification

Exchange Rates

📘 Key Terms
  • Nominal exchange rate ($e$): The price of one currency in terms of another (e.g., 25,000 VND per 1 USD).
  • Real exchange rate ($\varepsilon$): The relative price of goods between countries: $$\varepsilon = e \times \frac{P^*}{P}$$ where $P^*$ = foreign price level, $P$ = domestic price level. Measures competitiveness.
  • Appreciation: Currency becomes more valuable (e falls for VND/USD) → exports become more expensive, imports cheaper.
  • Depreciation: Currency becomes less valuable (e rises) → exports cheaper, imports more expensive.

Exchange Rate Regimes

RegimeDescriptionExample
Fixed (Peg)Government maintains exchange rate at a set levelHong Kong dollar pegged to USD
FloatingMarket supply/demand determine the rateUSD, EUR, JPY
Managed floatMostly market-determined but central bank intervenesVietnam (VND) — SBV manages within a band

Balance of Payments

📘 Balance of Payments (BOP)
Records all economic transactions between residents and the rest of the world.
  • Current Account: Trade in goods and services + income flows + transfers. CA surplus = exports > imports.
  • Capital/Financial Account: Investment flows (FDI, portfolio investment, loans). Capital inflows = foreigners investing in your country.
  • BOP identity: Current Account + Capital Account + Official Reserves Changes = 0
📊 The Open Economy Identity
$$Y = C + I + G + NX$$ $$\Rightarrow NX = Y - (C + I + G) = S - I$$ Insight: A trade deficit ($NX < 0$) means the country invests more than it saves ($I > S$), financed by foreign capital inflows. A trade surplus means the country saves more than it invests.

Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

💡 PPP Theory
In the long run, exchange rates should adjust so that identical goods cost the same in all countries (law of one price): $$e = \frac{P}{P^*}$$ PPP-adjusted GDP is often used for international comparisons because it accounts for differences in price levels. Vietnam's GDP per capita in PPP (~$16,000) is much higher than in nominal terms (~$4,300) because prices in Vietnam are lower than in developed countries.
