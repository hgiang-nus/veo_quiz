PART 1

Why Do Countries Trade?

Absolute vs Comparative Advantage

Absolute Advantage

A country can produce a good using fewer resources than another.

"Who is more productive?"

Comparative Advantage

A country can produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another.

"Who gives up less?"

📘 The Key Insight (David Ricardo, 1817)
Trade is based on comparative advantage, NOT absolute advantage. Even if one country is better at producing everything, both benefit from specialising in what they do relatively best.

📊 Comparative Advantage: Step-by-Step

Example (VEO Practice)

US: 150 wheat OR 50 steel. Vietnam: 40 wheat OR 20 steel.

OC of 1 SteelOC of 1 Wheat
US150/50 = 3 wheat50/150 = 1/3 steel ← lower ✓
Vietnam40/20 = 2 wheat ← lower ✓20/40 = 1/2 steel

Vietnam: CA in steel (OC = 2 wheat < 3 wheat)

US: CA in wheat (OC = 1/3 steel < 1/2 steel)

📝 Method: How to find CA
  1. Calculate opportunity cost for each good in each country
  2. Whoever has the lower OC has the comparative advantage in that good
  3. Each country specialises in its CA good and trades

Gains from Trade

Why both gain:

  1. Specialisation → each produces where relatively best → total world output ↑
  2. Consumption beyond PPF → through trade, countries consume combinations impossible alone
  3. Efficiency → resources allocated to highest-value use

Terms of Trade

The exchange ratio must lie between the two countries' opportunity costs for both to gain.

In our example: 1 steel trades for between 2 and 3 wheat.

If trade price = 2.5 wheat/steel: both gain (Vietnam gets wheat cheaper than making it; US gets steel cheaper).

🏆 VEO Practice: Comparative Advantage

Vietnam and US produce wheat and steel. US: 150 wheat or 50 steel. Vietnam: 40 wheat or 20 steel. Who should produce what?

  • (a) Cannot determine
  • (b) Both produce both
  • (c) Vietnam → steel, US → wheat ✓
  • (d) Vietnam → wheat, US → steel
Vietnam has CA in steel (lower OC: 2 < 3 wheat). US has CA in wheat (lower OC: 1/3 < 1/2 steel). Note: US has absolute advantage in BOTH goods (150>40, 50>20) — but trade still benefits both!

PART 3

Exchange Rates

Nominal Exchange Rate

📘 Definition
The nominal exchange rate (e) = price of one currency in terms of another.
Example: 1 USD = 25,000 VND → e = 25,000 VND/USD

Appreciation

Currency gains value.

e falls (fewer VND per USD).

Exports more expensive → NX↓

Imports cheaper

Depreciation

Currency loses value.

e rises (more VND per USD).

Exports cheaper → NX↑

Imports more expensive

Real Exchange Rate

$\varepsilon = e \times \dfrac{P^*}{P}$

$e$ = nominal rate, $P^*$ = foreign price level, $P$ = domestic price level

📘 What it measures
The relative price of goods between countries. Measures competitiveness.
  • Higher ε = domestic goods relatively expensive → less competitive → NX↓
  • Lower ε = domestic goods relatively cheap → more competitive → NX↑

Exchange Rate Regimes

RegimeHow it worksProsConsExample
Fixed (Peg)CB maintains rate at set levelStability, low uncertaintyLoses independent MP; needs large reservesHKD pegged to USD

Vietnam: SBV uses a managed float — allows VND to move within a band around a central rate, and intervenes using foreign reserves to prevent excessive volatility.

PART 4

Balance of Payments

Balance of Payments (BOP)

📘 Definition
Records all economic transactions between a country's residents and the rest of the world.
AccountRecordsExamples
Current AccountTrade in goods & services + income + transfersExports, imports, remittances, foreign aid
Capital/Financial AccountInvestment flowsFDI, portfolio investment, loans, bank deposits
Official ReservesCB transactions in foreign currencyForex reserves changes
📘 BOP Identity
$$\text{Current Account} + \text{Capital Account} + \Delta\text{Official Reserves} = 0$$The BOP always balances. A current account deficit must be financed by capital inflows (or reserve drawdown).

The Open Economy Identity

$NX = S - I$

Net exports = National saving − Investment

Trade Deficit (NX < 0)

$I > S$ → Country invests more than it saves → borrows from abroad (capital inflows).

Not necessarily bad — may reflect attractive investment opportunities.

Trade Surplus (NX > 0)

$S > I$ → Country saves more than it invests → lends to abroad (capital outflows).

Vietnam has had trade surpluses in recent years.

Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

📘 PPP Theory
In the long run, exchange rates adjust so identical goods cost the same everywhere (law of one price): $e = P/P^*$
📊 Why PPP matters

GDP per capita comparison:

  • Vietnam nominal GDP/capita: ~$4,300 (looks low)
  • Vietnam PPP GDP/capita: ~$16,193 (much higher!)

Prices in Vietnam are much lower than developed countries. PPP adjusts for this → gives a more accurate picture of living standards.

⚠️ PPP doesn't hold perfectly
Transport costs, trade barriers, non-traded goods (haircuts, housing), different quality → PPP is a long-run tendency, not an exact law.

🏆 Practice: Comparative Advantage Calculation

Country A: 100 cloth OR 50 wine. Country B: 80 cloth OR 40 wine.
Who has CA in what? What are the terms of trade?

OC of 1 WineOC of 1 Cloth
A100/50 = 2 cloth50/100 = 0.5 wine ← lower ✓
B80/40 = 2 cloth40/80 = 0.5 wine

Both have IDENTICAL opportunity costs → no comparative advantage → no gains from trade!

This is a trick question. CA requires different OCs.

⚠️ Common Exam Mistakes

❌ Don't
  • Confuse absolute with comparative advantage
  • Forget: CA is about opportunity cost, not who produces more
  • Mix up appreciation and depreciation effects
  • Say "trade deficit = bad" (not necessarily!)
  • Forget NX = S − I identity
✅ Do
  • Always calculate OC for BOTH goods in BOTH countries
  • State terms of trade range for gains from trade
  • Use real exchange rate for competitiveness analysis
  • Trace: exchange rate change → export/import prices → NX
  • Connect to Vietnam's FTAs and trade data
