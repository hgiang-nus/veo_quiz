Topic 1 of 9

Financial Planning

$ Core Concepts
  • Income: Earned (salary, wages) vs. Unearned (investments, rent, dividends)
  • Expenses: Fixed (rent, insurance) vs. Variable (food, entertainment)
  • Needs vs. Wants: Essential expenditures vs. discretionary spending
  • Net income: Gross income − Taxes − Deductions
B Budgeting
  • 50/30/20 Rule: 50% needs, 30% wants, 20% savings
  • Emergency fund: 3–6 months of expenses
  • Pay yourself first: Save before spending
  • Tracking: Monitor cash inflows vs. outflows
G Financial Goals
  • Short-term: < 1 year (emergency fund, vacation)
  • Medium-term: 1–5 years (car, education)
  • Long-term: > 5 years (retirement, home)
  • SMART goals: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound
D Debt Management
  • Debt-to-income ratio: Monthly debt payments / Gross monthly income
  • Avalanche method: Pay highest interest rate first
  • Snowball method: Pay smallest balance first
  • Good debt (mortgage, education) vs. Bad debt (credit card)

IEO Tip: Questions often test whether students can distinguish between income types and calculate net disposable income after taxes.

Topic 2 of 9

Banks & the Banking System

B Types of Financial Institutions
  • Commercial banks: Accept deposits, make loans, facilitate payments
  • Central bank: Controls monetary policy, issues currency, lender of last resort
  • Investment banks: Underwriting, M&A advisory, trading
  • Credit unions: Member-owned, non-profit cooperative
R Reserve System
  • Fractional reserve: Banks keep only a fraction of deposits as reserves
  • Reserve ratio (rr): Required reserves / Total deposits
  • Excess reserves: Actual − Required reserves → available for lending
Money Multiplier = 1 / rr
Max new money = Initial deposit × (1/rr)
C Central Bank Tools
  • Open market operations: Buy bonds → ↑ money supply; Sell bonds → ↓ money supply
  • Discount rate: Interest rate the CB charges commercial banks
  • Reserve requirements: ↑ rr → ↓ lending → ↓ money supply
D Deposits & Loans
  • Demand deposits: Withdrawable anytime (checking accounts)
  • Time deposits: Fixed term, higher interest rate
  • Secured loans: Backed by collateral (mortgage)
  • Unsecured loans: No collateral (credit card debt)

Key mechanism: When banks lend, they create new deposits → new money. When loans are repaid, money is destroyed. This is how the banking system expands and contracts the money supply.

Topic 3 of 9

Saving Money & Compound Interest

S Why Save?
  • Precautionary: Buffer against unexpected events (job loss, medical)
  • Life-cycle: Smooth consumption over lifetime (earn more now, spend in retirement)
  • Goal-oriented: Accumulate for specific purchases
  • Opportunity cost of saving: Foregone current consumption
T Types of Savings Accounts
  • Regular savings: Low interest, high liquidity
  • High-yield savings: Higher rate, may have restrictions
  • Certificate of Deposit (CD): Fixed term, penalty for early withdrawal
  • Money market: Higher minimums, check-writing ability
C Compound Interest

The "eighth wonder of the world" — earning interest on interest.

FV = PV × (1 + r)ⁿ

FV = Future Value, PV = Present Value, r = interest rate per period, n = number of periods

Rule of 72: Doubling time ≈ 72 / r%

At 6% interest, money doubles in ≈ 12 years.

E Example Calculation

Deposit 10,000 at 5% for 10 years:

Simple: 10,000 + (10,000 × 0.05 × 10) = 15,000

Compound: 10,000 × (1.05)¹⁰ = 16,289

Difference: 1,289 — the power of compounding!

IEO Tip: Expect calculation questions using compound interest. Practice computing (1+r)ⁿ without a calculator for small n.

Topic 4 of 9

Borrowing Money & Credit

C Credit Fundamentals
  • Credit: Borrowing money with a promise to repay + interest
  • Principal: Original amount borrowed
  • Interest rate: Cost of borrowing (annual %)
  • APR: Annual Percentage Rate — total cost including fees
  • Collateral: Asset pledged to secure a loan
T Types of Credit
  • Credit cards: Revolving credit, high interest (15–25% APR)
  • Mortgage: Home loan, lower rate, 15–30 year term
  • Auto loan: Secured by the vehicle
  • Student loans: Often subsidized, deferred payment
  • Payday loans: Short-term, extremely high APR — predatory
S Credit Score
  • Payment history (35%): On-time payments — most important factor
  • Amounts owed (30%): Credit utilization ratio
  • Length of history (15%): Longer = better
  • New credit (10%): Hard inquiries lower score temporarily
  • Credit mix (10%): Variety of credit types

Note: Income is NOT directly used in credit scoring.

L Loan Amortization
Monthly payment = P × [r(1+r)ⁿ] / [(1+r)ⁿ − 1]

Early payments are mostly interest; later payments are mostly principal. This is why extra payments early in a mortgage save the most.

Topic 5 of 9

Investment, Financial Instruments & Risk

T Time Value of Money

A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow.

PV = FV / (1 + r)ⁿ
NPV = −Cost + Σ [CFₜ / (1+r)ᵗ]

Accept investment if NPV > 0.

I Financial Instruments
  • Stocks: Ownership shares — dividends + capital gains. Higher risk/return
  • Bonds: Debt instruments — fixed coupon payments. Lower risk/return
  • Mutual funds: Pooled investments, professionally managed
  • ETFs: Trade like stocks, track an index
  • Derivatives: Options, futures — derive value from underlying assets
R Risk vs. Return
  • Fundamental tradeoff: Higher expected return ↔ Higher risk
  • Diversification: "Don't put all eggs in one basket" — reduces unsystematic risk
  • Systematic risk: Market-wide, cannot be diversified away (recessions)
  • Unsystematic risk: Firm-specific, can be diversified (management failure)
B Bond Valuation
Bond Price = Σ [Coupon/(1+r)ᵗ] + Face/(1+r)ⁿ
  • Coupon rate < Market rate → bond trades at discount
  • Coupon rate > Market rate → bond trades at premium
  • Bond prices and interest rates move inversely

IEO Tip: NPV and bond valuation calculations appear frequently. Practice the annuity PV formula: PV = C × [1 − (1+r)⁻ⁿ] / r

Topic 6 of 9

Insurance Contracts & Insurance Market

I Insurance Fundamentals
  • Premium: Regular payment for coverage
  • Coverage: Maximum amount insurer will pay
  • Deductible: Amount you pay before insurance kicks in
  • Copay: Fixed amount per service
  • Liability: Legal responsibility for damages/injury
  • Claim: Request for payment under the policy
T Types of Insurance
  • Health: Medical expenses coverage
  • Life: Payment to beneficiaries upon death
  • Auto: Vehicle damage and liability
  • Property/Home: Damage to real estate
  • Liability: Protection against third-party claims
E Economics of Insurance
  • Risk pooling: Spread risk across many policyholders
  • Actuarially fair premium: = Probability × Loss amount
  • Risk-averse people buy insurance (concave utility → prefer certainty)
  • Risk-neutral people are indifferent at fair premium
Fair Premium π = P(accident) × Loss
M Market Problems
  • Moral hazard: Insured take more risk after getting coverage (hidden action)
  • Adverse selection: High-risk individuals more likely to buy insurance (hidden info)
  • Solutions: Deductibles (co-insurance), screening, experience rating

Connection to utility theory: A risk-averse person (u'' < 0) always prefers insurance at the actuarially fair premium because, by Jensen's inequality, u(E[W]) > E[u(W)].

Topic 7 of 9

Financial Fraud & Ponzi Schemes

P Ponzi Scheme
  • Returns paid from new investors' money, not actual profits
  • Promises unusually high, consistent returns
  • Collapses when new investments can't cover payouts
  • Bernie Madoff (2008): Largest Ponzi scheme — $65 billion fraud

Key difference from pyramid: Ponzi has a central operator managing the fraud; pyramid requires each participant to recruit new members.

P Pyramid Scheme
  • Participants pay to join and earn by recruiting others
  • No legitimate product/service (or product is secondary)
  • Mathematically unsustainable — requires exponential growth
  • Early participants profit; later ones lose everything
D Digital Fraud
  • Phishing: Fake emails/websites to steal personal data
  • Identity theft: Using someone's personal info fraudulently
  • Advance fee fraud: "Pay upfront to receive a larger sum"
  • Pump and dump: Inflate stock price with false info, then sell
P Protection & Red Flags
  • Returns that are "too good to be true" (guaranteed high returns)
  • Unregistered investments or unlicensed sellers
  • Secretive or complex strategies
  • Difficulty receiving payments or cashing out
  • Pressure to recruit new investors
  • Regulatory measures: SEC, financial authorities, investor education

IEO Tip: Know the precise distinction between Ponzi and pyramid schemes. VEO 2021/2025 tested this directly.

Topic 8 of 9

Crowdfunding

C What is Crowdfunding?

Raising small amounts of money from a large number of people, typically via online platforms, to finance a project, business, or cause.

  • Democratizes access to capital
  • Bypasses traditional financial intermediaries
  • Leverages social networks and community engagement
T Types of Crowdfunding
  • Donation-based: No return expected (charity, disaster relief)
  • Reward-based: Backers receive a product/perk (Kickstarter)
  • Equity-based: Investors receive ownership shares
  • Debt-based (P2P lending): Lenders receive interest payments
P Major Platforms
  • Kickstarter: Reward-based, creative projects (all-or-nothing model)
  • Indiegogo: Reward-based, flexible funding option
  • GoFundMe: Donation-based, personal causes
  • SeedInvest, Crowdcube: Equity-based
  • LendingClub: Peer-to-peer debt
R Risks & Considerations
  • For backers: Project may fail, no guarantee of returns or delivery
  • For creators: May underestimate costs, face production delays
  • Regulatory: Equity crowdfunding is regulated (investor protection)
  • Information asymmetry: Backers have limited due diligence ability
Topic 9 of 9

Cryptocurrencies & Blockchain

B Blockchain Technology
  • Distributed ledger: Decentralized record of all transactions
  • Blocks: Groups of transactions linked cryptographically
  • Immutable: Once recorded, data cannot be altered
  • Consensus mechanisms: Proof of Work (mining), Proof of Stake
  • Key property: Trust without a central authority
C Key Cryptocurrencies
  • Bitcoin (BTC): First and largest by market cap, store of value
  • Ethereum (ETH): Smart contracts, programmable blockchain
  • Stablecoins: Pegged to fiat currency (USDT, USDC)
  • CBDCs: Central Bank Digital Currencies — government-backed digital money
R Risks & Misconceptions
  • Volatility: Extreme price swings — not a stable store of value
  • NOT anonymous: Pseudonymous — transactions are traceable on public blockchain
  • Environmental: Proof of Work consumes enormous energy
  • Regulatory uncertainty: Legal status varies by country
  • No consumer protection: Lost keys = lost funds, irreversible
D DeFi & Beyond
  • DeFi: Decentralized Finance — lending, borrowing without banks
  • Smart contracts: Self-executing agreements on blockchain
  • NFTs: Non-fungible tokens — unique digital ownership
  • Tokenization: Converting real-world assets to digital tokens

IEO Tip: The most common exam trap: "All crypto transactions are anonymous." This is FALSE — they are pseudonymous and traceable. VEO 2026 Mock tested this directly.

Summary & Exam Strategy

Format: 25 MCQs, 90 min (shared with Economics MCQs = 40 total). Correct +2, Wrong −0.5, Blank 0.

High-yield topics: Compound interest calculations, NPV/bond valuation, money multiplier, insurance economics (moral hazard vs adverse selection), credit scores.

Strategy: Skip questions you're unsure about (−0.5 penalty for wrong). Answer all questions where you can eliminate 2+ options. Time: ~2 min per question.

Common traps: Crypto is NOT anonymous. Income is NOT part of credit scores. Ponzi ≠ Pyramid. Simple ≠ Compound interest.
